Case 1Asthma Exacerbation
A 24-year-old woman with known asthma presents to the ED with worsening shortness of breath and wheezing for 6 hours. She has used her albuterol inhaler 8 times today with minimal relief. She uses fluticasone and salmeterol at home. Vitals: T 37.1C, BP 130/82, HR 118, RR 28, SpO2 91% on room air. Exam: accessory muscle use, prolonged expiration, diffuse expiratory wheezes. Peak flow 38% of predicted.
What is the most appropriate initial management?
A. Nebulized albuterol + ipratropium, systemic corticosteroids, supplemental O2
B. IV magnesium sulfate alone
C. Intubate immediately for respiratory failure
D. Oral prednisone and discharge with follow-up in 48 hours
Answer: A - Nebulized SABA + ipratropium + systemic steroids + O2

Severe asthma (SpO2 <92%, peak flow <40%, accessory muscle use): First-line = nebulized albuterol 2.5-5 mg q20min x3 (or continuous). Add ipratropium for severe exacerbations. Systemic corticosteroids (prednisone 40-60 mg PO or IV methylprednisolone) reduce inflammation - give within 1 hour. Supplemental O2 target SpO2 93-95%. IV Mg2+ (2g over 20min) reserved for life-threatening cases. Intubation is last resort. Key pearl: A "normal" PaCO2 in a severely wheezing patient is WORRISOME - it should be low due to hyperventilation. Rising PaCO2 = impending respiratory failure.
Case 2COPD Exacerbation
A 68-year-old man with 50-pack-year smoking history and COPD (GOLD Stage III) presents with 3 days of worsening dyspnea and sputum changing from white to yellow-green. He takes tiotropium and formoterol. Vitals: T 38.1C, BP 148/90, HR 102, RR 24, SpO2 85% on room air. ABG: pH 7.33, PaCO2 58, PaO2 52, HCO3 29. CXR: hyperinflation, no infiltrate.
What is the most appropriate oxygen target and antibiotic choice?
A. Target SpO2 98-100%; no antibiotics (likely viral)
B. Target SpO2 88-92%; azithromycin or doxycycline
C. Target SpO2 88-92%; no antibiotics needed
D. Target SpO2 94-98%; piperacillin-tazobactam IV
Answer: B - SpO2 88-92%; azithromycin or doxycycline

O2 target 88-92% in COPD (NOT 100%) - chronic CO2 retainers rely on hypoxic drive; excessive O2 worsens hypercapnia (Haldane effect + V/Q mismatch). ABG = type II respiratory failure with chronic compensation (elevated HCO3). Consider BiPAP if pH <7.35. Antibiotics indicated for purulent sputum change (Anthonisen criteria). Azithromycin x5 days or doxycycline = first-line. Reserve piperacillin-tazobactam for Pseudomonas risk factors (frequent exacerbations, structural lung disease, recent hospitalization). Also: SABA + SAMA nebulization + systemic steroids (prednisolone 40mg x5 days).
Case 3Pulmonary Embolism
A 38-year-old woman 6 weeks postpartum presents with sudden pleuritic chest pain and dyspnea. She had a cesarean section and has been sedentary. Vitals: T 37.4C, BP 104/68, HR 128, RR 26, SpO2 89%. ECG: sinus tachycardia, S1Q3T3. CXR: normal. D-dimer 8,400 ng/mL.
What is the most appropriate next step?
A. V/Q scan; start heparin only after imaging confirms PE
B. CTPA and start anticoagulation immediately
C. Lower extremity Doppler ultrasound; discharge with oral anticoagulation
D. Echocardiogram to rule out MI first
Answer: B - CTPA + immediate anticoagulation

High-probability PE (all of Virchow's triad: stasis postpartum/sedentary, hypercoagulability of pregnancy/postpartum, vessel injury from cesarean). CTPA = gold standard. Do NOT wait for imaging before anticoagulation in high-risk PE - start IV heparin or LMWH immediately. V/Q preferred if CTPA contraindicated (renal failure, contrast allergy, pregnancy). This patient is hemodynamically unstable - if deteriorates further, consider systemic thrombolysis (tPA) or catheter-directed therapy. S1Q3T3 = RV strain from massive PE. For submassive PE (normal BP, RV strain): anticoagulate + close monitoring.
Case 4Community-Acquired Pneumonia
A 72-year-old man with DM2, CHF (EF 40%), and CKD3 presents with 4 days of productive cough with rust-colored sputum, fever, rigors, and right-sided pleuritic pain. Vitals: T 39.2C, BP 96/60, HR 116, RR 24, SpO2 91%. CXR: right lower lobe lobar consolidation. WBC 19,200. BUN 28.
What is the most appropriate management?
A. Outpatient azithromycin monotherapy
B. Hospitalize; IV ceftriaxone + azithromycin
C. Hospitalize; levofloxacin monotherapy
D. ICU; vancomycin + piperacillin-tazobactam
Answer: B - Hospitalize; IV ceftriaxone + azithromycin

CURB-65: BUN >19=1, BP <90/60=1, Age >=65=1 → score 3 → hospitalize. Rust-colored sputum = classic Streptococcus pneumoniae (most common CAP pathogen). Standard inpatient CAP: beta-lactam (ceftriaxone 1-2g IV daily) + macrolide (azithromycin) covers typical AND atypical organisms. Respiratory fluoroquinolone monotherapy (levofloxacin) = acceptable alternative for non-ICU inpatients. Anti-MRSA/Pseudomonal coverage reserved for ICU admission with severe CAP (3+ minor ATS/IDSA criteria). This patient meets some criteria (BP 96/60, RR 24) but has only 2 major criteria threshold - reassess after fluid resuscitation.
Case 5Tension Pneumothorax
A 22-year-old male trauma patient was intubated in the field after an MVA. Shortly after intubation, BP drops from 110/70 to 70/40 mmHg, HR rises to 138 bpm, and SpO2 falls to 82% despite 100% FiO2. Exam: tracheal deviation to the RIGHT, absent breath sounds on the LEFT, distended neck veins. CXR is pending.
What is the immediate next step?
A. Reposition ETT (rule out right mainstem intubation)
B. Wait for CXR confirmation before intervening
C. Needle decompression at the LEFT 2nd intercostal space, midclavicular line
D. Pericardiocentesis for cardiac tamponade
Answer: C - Needle decompression left 2nd ICS, MCL

Tension pneumothorax = CLINICAL diagnosis - do NOT wait for CXR. Classic findings: hypotension + absent unilateral breath sounds + tracheal deviation AWAY from affected side (trachea goes right = tension on LEFT). JVD from impaired venous return = obstructive shock. Pathophysiology: trapped air increases intrapleural pressure → mediastinal shift → kinks SVC/IVC → cardiac arrest if untreated. Treat: needle thoracostomy at 2nd ICS MCL on AFFECTED (left) side → converts tension to open pneumothorax → insert chest tube (4th/5th ICS, AAL). Differentiate from cardiac tamponade: Beck's triad (hypotension, JVD, muffled heart sounds), NO absent breath sounds, trachea midline. Post-intubation deterioration = right mainstem intubation OR tension pneumothorax.
Case 6Lung Cancer - Paraneoplastic
A 64-year-old man with 45-pack-year smoking history presents with 3 months of hoarseness, 15-lb weight loss, and persistent cough. He reports right shoulder and arm pain. Exam: right-sided ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis. CXR: right apical mass with rib destruction. Serum Na 122.
What is the most likely diagnosis and associated syndrome?
A. Small cell lung cancer with SIADH
B. Pancoast tumor (squamous cell) with Horner syndrome
C. Adenocarcinoma with hypertrophic osteoarthropathy
D. Large cell carcinoma with Lambert-Eaton syndrome
Answer: B - Pancoast tumor with Horner syndrome

Pancoast (superior sulcus) tumor: apical lung mass invading brachial plexus and sympathetic chain. Classic triad: Horner syndrome (ptosis + miosis + anhidrosis = loss of sympathetics from superior cervical ganglion at T1), shoulder/arm pain (C8-T2), and apical mass with rib/vertebral destruction. Most commonly squamous cell carcinoma. Paraneoplastic associations: Squamous = hypercalcemia (PTHrP); central, cavitating. Small cell = SIADH (hyponatremia), ectopic ACTH (Cushing), Lambert-Eaton (anti-VGCC; proximal weakness improves with repetition - opposite of MG). Adenocarcinoma = peripheral, most common overall, non-smokers, EGFR/ALK mutations, hypertrophic osteoarthropathy. The Na=122 is a distracter - APICAL location + Horner = Pancoast. SIADH would be small cell (central hilar mass).
Case 7ARDS - Lung-Protective Ventilation
A 55-year-old man is in the ICU for septic shock from CAP. On day 2, he is intubated. ABG on FiO2 1.0: pH 7.28, PaO2 62, PaCO2 48. CXR: bilateral alveolar infiltrates, no cardiomegaly. Echo: normal LV, PCWP ~12 mmHg. Current vent settings: TV 550 mL (IBW 70 kg), RR 16, PEEP 5.
Which ventilator change is most consistent with evidence-based ARDS management?
A. Increase tidal volume to 700 mL to improve oxygenation
B. Reduce tidal volume to 420 mL (6 mL/kg IBW) and increase PEEP
C. Maintain current settings and add inhaled nitric oxide
D. Switch to pressure control at 30 cmH2O driving pressure
Answer: B - Reduce TV to 6 mL/kg IBW + increase PEEP

ARDS Berlin criteria: acute onset, bilateral opacities, not fully cardiac, PaO2/FiO2: mild 200-300, moderate 100-200, severe <100. This patient: P/F = 62/1.0 = 62 = SEVERE ARDS. ARDSNet lung-protective ventilation (reduces mortality): TV 6 mL/kg IBW = 420 mL for 70 kg IBW. Plateau pressure target <30 cmH2O. Higher PEEP prevents atelectrauma. Permissive hypercapnia acceptable (pH >=7.20). Refractory hypoxemia strategies: prone positioning 12-16h/day (reduces mortality when P/F <150), neuromuscular blockade first 48h in severe ARDS. Inhaled NO improves oxygenation transiently but does NOT reduce mortality. Conservative fluids after resuscitation. High TV = volutrauma = worsens ARDS outcomes.
