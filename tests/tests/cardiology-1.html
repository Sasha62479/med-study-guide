Question 1
A 58-year-old with hypertension and type 2 diabetes presents with BP 158/96 on two separate visits. His current medications do not include an antihypertensive. What is the MOST appropriate first-line medication?
  • A. Metoprolol
  • B. Lisinopril
  • C. Amlodipine
  • D. Hydrochlorothiazide
Correct: B. Lisinopril (ACE inhibitor). In patients with hypertension AND diabetes, ACE inhibitors (or ARBs) are first-line due to renoprotective effects — they reduce proteinuria and slow CKD progression. JNC 8 and ADA guidelines support this. If ACE-I is not tolerated (cough), switch to an ARB (e.g., losartan).
Question 2
A 70-year-old with a history of heart failure (EF 35%) presents with worsening dyspnea, orthopnea, and +2 pitting edema to the knees. Which medication has been shown to REDUCE MORTALITY in HFrEF?
  • A. Furosemide
  • B. Digoxin
  • C. Carvedilol
  • D. Amlodipine
Correct: C. Carvedilol (beta-blocker). In HFrEF, mortality-reducing medications are: ACE-I/ARB, beta-blockers (carvedilol, metoprolol succinate, bisoprolol), and aldosterone antagonists (spironolactone). Furosemide relieves symptoms but does NOT reduce mortality. Digoxin reduces hospitalizations but not mortality. Amlodipine is not first-line in HF.
Question 3
A 62-year-old male presents with crushing substernal chest pain radiating to the left arm, diaphoresis, and nausea for 45 minutes. ECG shows ST elevation in leads II, III, and aVF. What is the MOST likely diagnosis?
  • A. Anterior STEMI
  • B. Unstable angina
  • C. Inferior STEMI
  • D. NSTEMI
Correct: C. Inferior STEMI. ST elevation in leads II, III, and aVF = inferior wall MI, typically from RCA (right coronary artery) occlusion. Anterior STEMI involves V1-V4 (LAD territory). NSTEMI has no ST elevation (ST depression or T-wave changes). Management: activate cath lab immediately, aspirin + P2Y12 inhibitor, heparin, primary PCI within 90 minutes.
Question 4
A 72-year-old woman with hypertension has an irregularly irregular heart rate of 88 bpm on exam. ECG shows no discrete P waves and an irregularly irregular ventricular rhythm. She has no contraindications to anticoagulation. What is the BEST next step?
  • A. Aspirin only
  • B. Rate control with metoprolol only
  • C. Calculate CHA2DS2-VASc score and initiate anticoagulation
  • D. Immediate cardioversion
Correct: C. CHA2DS2-VASc and anticoagulation. This is atrial fibrillation. For stroke risk, calculate CHA2DS2-VASc (1 point each: CHF, HTN, age 65-74, DM, vascular disease, female; 2 pts: age ≥75, prior stroke). Score ≥2 in men or ≥3 in women warrants anticoagulation (DOACs preferred over warfarin). Aspirin alone is no longer recommended for AF stroke prevention.
Question 5
A patient is prescribed hydrochlorothiazide for hypertension. Two weeks later, lab work shows potassium 3.1 mEq/L. What is the MOST appropriate next step?
  • A. Stop HCTZ immediately and switch to amlodipine
  • B. Add spironolactone
  • C. Supplement potassium and dietary counseling; continue HCTZ
  • D. No action needed, this is expected
Correct: C. Thiazide diuretics cause hypokalemia by increasing urinary K+ excretion. K+ of 3.1 is mildly low (normal 3.5-5.0). First step: oral potassium supplementation + dietary counseling (bananas, oranges, leafy greens). HCTZ does not need to be stopped for mild hypokalemia. If K+ is severely low or symptomatic, consider adding an ACE-I or spironolactone.
Question 6
A 55-year-old with known CAD presents for follow-up. His LDL is 88 mg/dL on high-intensity statin therapy. According to ACC/AHA guidelines, what is the LDL goal for a patient with established ASCVD?
  • A. LDL < 130 mg/dL
  • B. LDL < 100 mg/dL
  • C. LDL < 70 mg/dL
  • D. LDL < 55 mg/dL
Correct: C. LDL < 70 mg/dL for very high-risk ASCVD (established CAD, prior MI, stroke). Some newer ESC guidelines suggest <55 for very-high-risk, but the standard ACC/AHA threshold for secondary prevention is <70 mg/dL. If not at goal on max-dose statin, add ezetimibe, then PCSK9 inhibitor.
Question 7
A 66-year-old with HTN, DM, and HFrEF (EF 30%) develops a persistent dry cough after starting lisinopril. What is the BEST alternative?
  • A. Amlodipine
  • B. Metoprolol succinate
  • C. Losartan (ARB)
  • D. Hydrochlorothiazide
Correct: C. Losartan (ARB). ACE inhibitor-induced cough (bradykinin accumulation) affects ~15% of patients. ARBs block the angiotensin II receptor and do NOT cause bradykinin accumulation — no cough. ARBs provide the same mortality benefit in HFrEF and renoprotection in DM. Do NOT combine ACE-I + ARB (doubles side effects without benefit).
Question 8
A 45-year-old otherwise healthy patient is found to have BP 148/92 mmHg on three separate occasions. He exercises regularly and does not smoke. What is the FIRST-LINE management?
  • A. Immediate pharmacotherapy with lisinopril
  • B. Refer to cardiologist
  • C. Lifestyle modification for 3-6 months before medication
  • D. 24-hour ambulatory blood pressure monitoring only
Correct: C. Lifestyle modification first. For Stage 1 HTN (130-139/80-89) in low-risk patients (no ASCVD, DM, CKD, or 10-year risk <10%), lifestyle modification for 3-6 months is appropriate before starting medication. This includes: DASH diet, reduced sodium (<2.3g/day), exercise 150 min/week, weight loss, alcohol reduction, smoking cessation.
Question 9
A 60-year-old presents with sudden onset palpitations. ECG shows a narrow-complex tachycardia at 175 bpm with no visible P waves. BP is 110/70 and patient is stable. What is the FIRST-LINE acute treatment?
  • A. Synchronized cardioversion
  • B. IV amiodarone
  • C. Vagal maneuvers, then IV adenosine
  • D. IV metoprolol
Correct: C. Vagal maneuvers then adenosine. This describes SVT (supraventricular tachycardia), likely AVNRT. For stable SVT: (1) Vagal maneuvers first (Valsalva, carotid massage), (2) IV adenosine 6mg rapid push if unresponsive. Adenosine transiently blocks the AV node, terminating the re-entry circuit. Cardioversion is reserved for unstable SVT.
Question 10
A patient with chronic stable angina is started on a nitrate. He is later prescribed sildenafil by another provider for erectile dysfunction. What is the PRIMARY concern?
  • A. Increased risk of MI
  • B. QT prolongation
  • C. Severe hypotension
  • D. Hyperkalemia
Correct: C. Severe hypotension. Nitrates + PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil, tadalafil, vardenafil) are ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED together. Both cause vasodilation; combined, they can cause life-threatening hypotension. This is a critical drug interaction to know for boards. If a patient needs treatment for both angina and ED, consider switching from nitrates to a different antianginal (e.g., beta-blocker, CCB).
