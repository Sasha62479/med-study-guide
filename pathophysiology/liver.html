Cirrhosis

Pathophysiology

Cirrhosis is the end-stage of chronic liver injury characterized by diffuse hepatic fibrosis and nodular regeneration that disrupts normal lobular architecture. Repeated hepatocyte injury activates hepatic stellate cells (Ito cells), which transdifferentiate into myofibroblasts and deposit collagen (especially type I and III), replacing functional parenchyma with scar tissue.

Etiology

  • Alcohol: most common in US; direct hepatotoxicity + acetaldehyde-mediated oxidative stress
  • NAFLD/NASH: rapidly increasing; associated with metabolic syndrome
  • Chronic viral hepatitis: HBV, HCV
  • Autoimmune hepatitis, PBC, PSC
  • Hemochromatosis, Wilson's disease, alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency

NAFLD / NASH

Spectrum of Disease

Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) ranges from simple steatosis → non-alcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH) → fibrosis → cirrhosis → HCC. NAFLD is the most common liver disease in the US, affecting ~25% of adults, and is strongly associated with metabolic syndrome (obesity, T2DM, hypertension, dyslipidemia).

Pathophysiology — "Two-Hit" Model

  • Hit 1: Insulin resistance → increased FFA delivery to liver → hepatic steatosis (triglyceride accumulation)
  • Hit 2: Oxidative stress, mitochondrial dysfunction, gut-derived endotoxins, adipokine imbalance → hepatocyte injury, inflammation, stellate cell activation → fibrosis

🎯 Boards PearlDiagnosis of NAFLD requires exclusion of significant alcohol use (<21 drinks/week men, <14 drinks/week women). Gold standard = liver biopsy; non-invasive alternatives include FibroScan (elastography) and FIB-4 score. First-line treatment: weight loss (7–10% body weight reduces steatosis and inflammation).

Portal Hypertension

Definition & Pathophysiology

Portal hypertension is defined as a portal venous pressure >5 mmHg above IVC pressure (normal <5 mmHg; clinically significant ≥10 mmHg; varices form ≥10 mmHg; variceal bleeding risk ≥12 mmHg). In cirrhosis, fibrosis increases intrahepatic vascular resistance, and splanchnic vasodilation (via NO, prostacyclin) increases portal blood flow — both elevate portal pressure.

Hepatic Encephalopathy

Pathophysiology

Hepatic encephalopathy (HE) results from accumulation of neurotoxins (primarily ammonia) that bypass hepatic detoxification through portosystemic shunts or due to hepatocyte dysfunction. Ammonia is produced by gut bacteria and intestinal glutaminase activity; normally converted to urea in the liver. In HE, ammonia crosses the blood-brain barrier → astrocyte swelling (Alzheimer type II astrocytes), cerebral edema, GABAergic neurotransmission enhancement, and altered neurotransmitter ratios.

Precipitating Factors & Treatment

🧠 Mnemonic — Precipitants: MASHEDMedications (opioids, benzos, diuretics) · Ammonia load (GI bleed, high protein) · Sepsis/infection · Hypokalemia/electrolytes · Excessive diuresis · Dehydration
🏥 ManagementIdentify and treat precipitant. Lactulose (1st line) — reduces ammonia by acidifying colonic pH, trapping NH4+, and acting as cathartic (goal 2–3 soft stools/day). Rifaximin (add-on or maintenance) — non-absorbable antibiotic reduces ammonia-producing gut bacteria. Dietary protein restriction is generally NOT recommended (maintain adequate nutrition).
