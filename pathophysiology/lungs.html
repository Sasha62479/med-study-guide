Asthma

Pathophysiology

Asthma is a chronic inflammatory airway disease characterized by episodic, reversible airway obstruction, bronchial hyperresponsiveness, and airway remodeling. Key mechanism: Th2-mediated inflammation → mast cell degranulation (histamine, leukotrienes, prostaglandins) → bronchospasm, mucosal edema, increased mucus secretion. Chronic inflammation → subepithelial fibrosis, smooth muscle hypertrophy, goblet cell hyperplasia (remodeling).

COPD

Pathophysiology

COPD is a progressive, largely irreversible airflow limitation caused by airway and/or alveolar damage from noxious particles or gases (smoking #1, alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency). Two main phenotypes: Chronic bronchitis (productive cough ≥3 months/year for ≥2 consecutive years; mucus hypersecretion, airway inflammation) and Emphysema (destruction of alveolar walls → loss of elastic recoil → air trapping → hyperinflation).

🏥 COPD ManagementSmoking cessation (#1 intervention to slow progression). Bronchodilators: SABA (albuterol) PRN → LAMA (tiotropium) or LABA → LAMA + LABA → add ICS. Phosphodiesterase-4 inhibitor (roflumilast) for frequent exacerbators with chronic bronchitis. Supplemental O2 if SaO2 ≤88% at rest (only intervention improving mortality). Pulmonary rehab. Vaccinate (influenza, pneumococcal, COVID-19). COPD exacerbation: nebulized bronchodilators, systemic steroids (5-day prednisone), antibiotics if purulent sputum (azithromycin, doxycycline, amoxicillin-clavulanate). Avoid high-flow O2 (hypoxic drive).

Pulmonary Embolism

Pathophysiology

PE occurs when a thrombus (usually from DVT in proximal leg veins) embolizes to the pulmonary vasculature. Virchow's Triad: venous stasis + hypercoagulability + endothelial injury. Massive PE → acute right ventricular failure (RV afterload overload → RV dilation, interventricular septal shift → reduced LV preload → hemodynamic collapse).

🧠 Mnemonic — ECG in PE: S1Q3T3S wave in lead I · Q wave in lead III · T-wave inversion in lead III. Most common ECG findings: sinus tachycardia, right heart strain (RAD, RBB, RBBB, T inversion V1–V4). Normal ECG in PE does not exclude the diagnosis.
🏥 TreatmentHemodynamically stable: anticoagulation (LMWH, UFH, or DOAC — rivaroxaban or apixaban preferred). Hemodynamically unstable (massive PE): systemic thrombolytics (tPA) or surgical/catheter-directed embolectomy. Long-term anticoagulation: 3 months if provoked; indefinite if unprovoked or hypercoagulable state. IVC filter only if anticoagulation absolutely contraindicated.

Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS)

Berlin Definition Criteria

  • Acute onset (within 1 week of known insult)
  • Bilateral opacities on CXR not fully explained by effusion, atelectasis, or nodules
  • Not fully explained by cardiac failure or fluid overload
  • PaO2/FiO2 ratio: Mild 201–300; Moderate 101–200; Severe ≤100 (all with PEEP ≥5 cmH2O)

Pathophysiology

Diffuse alveolar damage (DAD): direct (pneumonia, aspiration, inhalation) or indirect (sepsis, trauma, pancreatitis, transfusion/TRALI) insult → neutrophil activation → endothelial and epithelial injury → protein-rich edema → hyaline membranes → impaired gas exchange → refractory hypoxemia.

🏥 Management — ARDSnet ProtocolLow tidal volume ventilation: 6 mL/kg ideal body weight (lung-protective: prevents ventilator-induced lung injury). Plateau pressure ≤30 cmH2O. Permissive hypercapnia (allow pH 7.2–7.45). PEEP to optimize oxygenation. Prone positioning >16 hours/day (reduces mortality in severe ARDS — PaO2/FiO2 <150). Conservative fluid strategy (avoid excess fluids). Neuromuscular blockade (cisatracurium) for severe ARDS. Corticosteroids: methylprednisolone reduces duration of mechanical ventilation. Prone positioning reduces 28-day mortality by 16% in severe ARDS (PROSEVA trial).
