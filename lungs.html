Respiratory System

Airways & Lungs

The respiratory system consists of a conducting zone (anatomic dead space) and a respiratory zone (gas exchange). The right lung has 3 lobes; the left lung has 2 lobes with a lingula.

1. Zones Overview

ZoneStructuresFunction
Conducting zone (anatomic dead space)Mouth, nose, pharynx, larynx, trachea, bronchi, bronchioles, terminal bronchiolesAir conduction only; no gas exchange; warms, humidifies, and filters air
Respiratory zoneRespiratory bronchioles, alveolar ducts, alveolar sacs, alveoliO2 and CO2 exchange; site of gas exchange

Location

  • Lungs in thoracic cavity; surrounded by visceral pleura
  • Between lungs: inferior mediastinum (heart, large vessels, tracheal bifurcation, esophagus)
  • Superior: superior mediastinum; Inferior: diaphragm; Anterior and Posterior: ribs and vertebral column

Hilum of the Lung

  • Entry/exit site for: primary bronchi, pulmonary arteries and veins, bronchial arteries and veins, pulmonary nervous plexus, and lymphatics

Bronchopulmonary Segments

  • Supplied by a segmental bronchus and branch of pulmonary artery
  • Surrounded by a connective tissue septum (intersegmental septum)
  • Each segment can be surgically removed independently

3. Airways (Conducting Zone)

Trachea

  • 15-20 C-shaped hyaline cartilage rings joined by annular ligaments
  • Bifurcates at level of T4 into left and right main bronchi (carina)
  • Posterior: esophagus and descending aorta; Anterior: thyroid, ascending aorta, brachiocephalic trunk, SVC

Bronchi

  • Primary (main) bronchi: right (3 lobar branches) and left (2 lobar branches)
  • Lobar bronchi (secondary) divide into segmental bronchi (tertiary)
  • Supported by hyaline cartilage plates (unlike rings in trachea)

Bronchioles and Terminal Bronchioles

  • No cartilage; smooth muscle only
  • Function: conduction, anatomic dead space, mucociliary clearance
  • Terminal bronchioles: last segment of conducting zone before respiratory zone

Alveoli (Type I pneumocytes)Thin squamous (95% of alveolar area)Gas exchange; form blood-air barrier with capillary endothelium and basement membrane
Alveoli (Type II pneumocytes)Cuboidal (5% area, 60% of cells)Produce surfactant (DPPC/lecithin); can proliferate to replace type I or II after damage

Surfactant

  • Produced by type II pneumocytes via lamellar bodies
  • Main component: dipalmitoylphosphatidylcholine (DPPC/lecithin) and phosphatidylglycerol
  • Reduces alveolar surface tension, prevents alveolar collapse at end-expiration, increases compliance
Surfactant production: Begins at 20-22 weeks gestation; mature levels reached at 35 weeks. Corticosteroids accelerate lung development and surfactant production.

Alveolar Macrophages

  • Phagocytose foreign particles and initiate immune response
  • Produce alveolar elastase (degraded by TIMPs)
  • Hemosiderin-laden macrophages (heart failure cells) indicate pulmonary edema or alveolar hemorrhage

Inhaled Particle Clearance

  • Small particles (less than 3 um): reach alveoli; cleared by alveolar macrophages
  • Medium particles (3-10 um): deposit in trachea/bronchi; cleared by mucociliary action
  • Large particles (10+ um): deposit in nasal cavity; cleared by nasal hair (vibrissae)

5. Vasculature

The lungs have a dual blood supply: pulmonary circulation (gas exchange) and bronchial circulation (lung tissue nutrition).

Pulmonary Circulation

  • Pulmonary trunk: from right ventricle; divides into left and right pulmonary arteries; carries deoxygenated blood
  • Left pulmonary artery: superior to left main bronchus; connected to aorta by ligamentum arteriosum
  • Right pulmonary artery: posterior to aorta and SVC; anterior to right main bronchus
  • Pulmonary veins: 2 per lung (4 total) draining into left atrium; carry oxygenated blood

Bronchial Circulation

  • Bronchial arteries: left (2 from thoracic aorta); right (1 from right posterior intercostal or thoracic aorta)
  • Supply oxygenated blood to lung parenchyma, bronchi, and visceral pleura
  • Bronchial veins: right drains to azygos vein; left drains to accessory hemiazygos or superior intercostal vein

Lymphatics and Innervation

  • Lymphatics: intrapulmonary to bronchopulmonary to tracheobronchial to paratracheal to bronchomediastinal nodes to thoracic duct (left) or right lymphatic duct
  • Parasympathetic (vagus, M3): bronchoconstriction, increased secretion
  • Sympathetic (beta2): bronchodilation, vasoconstriction, decreased secretion

7. Clinical Significance

Obstructive Lung Diseases

  • Asthma, COPD (chronic bronchitis and emphysema), Bronchiectasis, Cystic fibrosis

Restrictive Lung Diseases

  • Interstitial lung disease, Pulmonary fibrosis, Sarcoidosis, Pneumoconiosis

Infectious

  • Pneumonia, Tuberculosis, Lung abscess, Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia

Vascular

  • Pulmonary embolism, Pulmonary hypertension, ARDS

Neonatal

  • Neonatal respiratory distress syndrome (surfactant deficiency), Tracheoesophageal fistula, Pulmonary hypoplasia, Bronchogenic cysts

Neoplastic

  • Lung cancer (adenocarcinoma, squamous cell, small cell, large cell), Mesothelioma

Other

  • Pneumothorax, Pleural effusion, Atelectasis, Pulmonary edema, Foreign body aspiration (right lung more common)
