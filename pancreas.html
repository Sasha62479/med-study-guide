Anatomy & Physiology

Pancreas

A retroperitoneal glandular organ with both exocrine (digestive enzymes) and endocrine (insulin, glucagon) functions, derived from the foregut endoderm.

1. Gross Anatomy

Overview

  • Glandular organ; Length: 12-15 cm; Weight: ~80 g
  • Location: retroperitoneal, between duodenal curvature and splenic hilum at level L1/L2
  • Secondary retroperitoneal; caudal to omental bursa

Pancreatic Ducts

  • Duct of Wirsung (main): extends from tail to head; joins CBD to form ampulla of Vater; empties at major duodenal papilla
  • Sphincter of Oddi: controls bile and pancreatic fluid release into duodenum
  • Duct of Santorini (accessory): drains head and body; empties at minor duodenal papilla
Clinical: Pancreatic head tumors often obstruct the bile duct causing painless jaundice (Courvoisier sign). Gallstones blocking ampulla of Vater cause both pancreatitis and cholangitis (double duct sign).

Vasculature

  • Head and neck arteries: superior pancreaticoduodenal (from gastroduodenal artery) and inferior pancreaticoduodenal (from SMA)
  • Body and tail arteries: branches of splenic artery (from celiac trunk)
  • Veins: pancreatic veins to SMV or splenic vein to portal vein
  • Lymphatics: celiac, SMA, and splenic lymph nodes to paraaortic nodes
  • Innervation: celiac ganglia (sympathetic T6-12; parasympathetic vagus nerve)

2. Microscopic Anatomy

Exocrine Compartment (more than 90% of pancreas)

  • Serous glandular tissue separated into lobules by collagenous septae
  • Pancreatic acini: secretory acinar cells surrounding a lumen; secrete proenzymes (e.g., trypsinogen) into intercalated ducts
  • Centroacinar cells: pale cells in center of acini; secrete bicarbonate into pancreatic fluid
  • EM appearance: basophilic rER at basal pole; eosinophilic zymogen granules at apical pole
Histology pearl: The exocrine pancreas resembles salivary glands but lacks myoepithelial cells and striated ducts. Centroacinar cells are unique to the pancreas.

Endocrine Compartment - Islets of Langerhans

  • Alpha cells (periphery): glucagon
  • Beta cells (center/INSide): INSulin
  • Delta cells: somatostatin
  • Epsilon cells: ghrelin
  • PP cells: pancreatic polypeptide
Mnemonic: Beta cells are on the INSide of islets and produce INSulin.
Boards pearl: Most pancreatic malignancies are adenocarcinomas originating in the ductal epithelium.

3. Exocrine Pancreas

The pancreatic fluid is isotonic and contains digestive enzymes, bicarbonate, and electrolytes. Secreted as inactive proenzymes (zymogens) to prevent autodigestion.

Regulation

  • Stimulation: CCK (from duodenum), Secretin (from duodenum)
  • Inhibition: Somatostatin
Acute pancreatitis: Premature activation of pancreatic enzymes within ducts causes autodigestion. Serum lipase and amylase are elevated.
Cystic fibrosis: Defective Cl- channel impairs water/electrolyte secretion, causing ductal obstruction and maldigestion/malabsorption.

Insulin Secretion Pathway

  • Glucose enters beta-cells via GLUT2
  • Glucokinase converts glucose to glucose-6-phosphate
  • Increased ATP (from glycolysis) closes ATP-sensitive K+ channels
  • Membrane depolarization opens voltage-gated Ca2+ channels
  • Ca2+ influx triggers insulin exocytosis

Insulin Synthesis

  • Insulin gene transcription and translation in rER of beta-cells produces preproinsulin
  • Signal peptide removed = proinsulin; transported to Golgi, packaged in granules
  • Proteases cleave proinsulin = mature insulin + C-peptide (secreted equimolarly)
C-peptide: Absent in exogenous insulin. Used to distinguish endogenous (insulinoma, sulfonylurea use) from exogenous hyperinsulinism.
Oral vs IV glucose: Oral glucose stimulates MORE insulin release than IV due to incretin (GLP-1, GIP) secretion from the gut.

5. Embryology

  • Origin: endoderm of the foregut
  • Ventral pancreatic bud: forms main pancreatic duct, uncinate process, lower head
  • Dorsal pancreatic bud: forms rest of head, neck, body, tail, and accessory duct
  • Foregut rotation causes ventral bud to fuse with dorsal bud then secondary retroperitonealization

Congenital Anomalies

AnomalyMechanismPresentation
Annular pancreasFailure of ventral bud rotation - ring of pancreas around 2nd part of duodenumPolyhydramnios; bilious or non-bilious vomiting; double bubble sign on X-ray; associated with Down syndrome
Pancreas divisumFailure of ventral and dorsal buds to fuse by week 8 (most common congenital defect)Usually asymptomatic; may cause chronic abdominal pain or pancreatitis

6. Clinical Significance

Exocrine Disorders

  • Cystic fibrosis, Acute pancreatitis, Chronic pancreatitis
  • Pancreatic cancer (adenocarcinoma), Gallstone pancreatitis
  • Cholangitis, Mumps, Pancreatic pseudocyst

Endocrine Disorders

  • Diabetes mellitus (Type 1 and Type 2)
  • Pancreatic neuroendocrine tumors: Insulinoma, Gastrinoma (Zollinger-Ellison syndrome), Glucagonoma, Somatostatinoma, VIPoma
  • MEN 1 syndrome, Hemochromatosis

Embryological Anomalies

  • Annular pancreas, Pancreas divisum
