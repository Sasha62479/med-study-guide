Pediatric Murmurs
Story-based analogies, a fast clinical approach, and board-style practice cases to make murmur patterns stick.

Heart Murmur Master Image

Think of the heart like a house with doors and pipes. Valves are doors. Blood flow is people moving through rooms. Murmurs are traffic problems. Tight doors create pushing sounds. Leaky doors create backflow sounds. Holes create hissing. A machine that never turns off is PDA.

Fast Rules

  • Systolic usually means stenosis or regurgitation
  • Diastolic is pathologic until proven otherwise
  • Continuous should make you think PDA first
  • Musical in a healthy child often means innocent murmur
Analogies

Aortic Stenosis

Systolic • RUSB • radiates to carotids
Trying to blow air through a tiny coffee stirrer

Blood is forced out through a very tight exit. Think harsh, squeezing, and pressure building before it escapes.

HarshCrescendo-decrescendoTight door

Pulmonic Stenosis

Systolic • LUSB
Kids trying to run out through a narrow playground gate

Same traffic jam idea as aortic stenosis, but on the right side of the heart.

LUSBOutflowStenosis

Mitral Regurgitation

Holosystolic • Apex • radiates to axilla
A door that will not close, so people leak back into the room

During systole, blood should go forward. Instead, some slips backward into the left atrium the whole time.

BlowingBackflowLeaky door

Ventricular Septal Defect

Holosystolic • LLSB
A small drill hole in the wall between two rooms

Pressure shoots through a septal hole and creates a sharp hissing sound. Small VSDs can be surprisingly loud.

HissLLSBSmall hole louder

Still's Murmur

Innocent • musical • often LLSB
A soft wind chime in an open window

Vibratory and musical, often heard in healthy children. The child otherwise looks well.

Aortic Regurgitation

Early diastolic • left sternal border
A broken faucet dripping back into the ventricle

After blood leaves, it returns because the valve does not seal.

Patent Ductus Arteriosus

Continuous • infraclavicular / left upper chest
A machine that never turns off

The sound keeps going in systole and diastole because flow continues through the ductus.

30-Second Board Approach

1. Timing

  • Systolic: stenosis or regurgitation are most likely
  • Diastolic: stop and treat as pathologic
  • Continuous: think PDA first

2. Sound

  • Harsh crescendo-decrescendo: stenosis
  • Blowing holosystolic: MR or VSD
  • Musical: Still's murmur

3. Location + Radiation

  • RUSB: aortic
  • LUSB: pulmonic
  • LLSB: VSD or Still's
  • Apex to axilla: mitral regurgitation

Red Flags

  • Any diastolic murmur
  • Cyanosis or syncope
  • Failure to thrive
  • Weak or unequal pulses
Practice Cases

Case 1

A 6-year-old has a harsh crescendo-decrescendo systolic murmur at the right upper sternal border that radiates to the carotids.

Answer: Aortic stenosis

Case 2

A toddler has a continuous murmur heard best just under the clavicle.

Answer: PDA

Case 3

A healthy 4-year-old has a soft musical murmur at the left lower sternal border that becomes quieter when standing.

Answer: Still's murmur

Case 4

An infant has a loud holosystolic murmur at the left lower sternal border.

Answer: VSD

Case 5

A blowing holosystolic murmur is heard at the apex and radiates to the axilla.

Answer: Mitral regurgitation

Case 6

An early diastolic decrescendo murmur is heard with bounding pulses.

Answer: Aortic regurgitation
