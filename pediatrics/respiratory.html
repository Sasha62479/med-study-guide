Acute Airway Disease
Localize the problem first: is it above or below the thoracic inlet? Stridor = upper. Wheeze = lower.
High Yield
🗣️
Upper Airway
Inspiratory stridor · barky cough · drooling = the danger sign
1.
Croup (Laryngotracheitis)
Parainfluenza · barky "seal" cough · steeple sign · dex + epi if severe
 2.
Epiglottitis
Hib (now rare) · tripod, drooling, toxic · do NOT examine throat · OR intubation
 3.
Bacterial Tracheitis
S. aureus · looks like severe croup not responding to tx · ICU + abx
 4.
Foreign Body Aspiration
Toddler · sudden onset · unilateral wheeze · bronchoscopy
 5.
Retropharyngeal Abscess
Neck stiffness · refuses to extend neck · lateral neck XR widens

✨ Board Pearls
Don't Miss These
Don't examine the throat
Toxic child, drooling, tripod posture → suspect epiglottitis. Leave the child with a parent, call anesthesia, go to OR. A tongue blade can cause full obstruction.
Bronchiolitis treatment
Supportive care only. No routine albuterol, no steroids, no antibiotics. This is the most tested "treatment = none" question in peds.
Silent chest
In a wheezing child, loss of audible wheeze is ominous, not reassuring. Means too little air is moving to make sound. Call PICU.
Croup severity
Stridor at rest = moderate-severe. Give racemic epi + observe 3–4 hrs (rebound risk) + dexamethasone. Mild = dex alone.
Pneumonia bugs by age
Neonate: GBS, E. coli · 1 mo–5 yr: viral > Strep pneumo · School age: Mycoplasma, Strep pneumo · Any age with Pseudomonas/Staph = think CF.
Unilateral wheeze
Focal, one-sided wheeze in a toddler = foreign body until proven otherwise. Inspiratory/expiratory CXR or fluoroscopy. Bronchoscopy to retrieve.
