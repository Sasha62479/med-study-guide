Structural Anomalies

Anatomical alterations causing functional obstruction — all require surgical referral if confirmed.

Pyloric Stenosis
Infantile Hypertrophic Pyloric Stenosis · Structural / Surgical
Domain Details
Pathophysiology Progressive hypertrophy of the pylorus (junction of stomach → duodenum) → gastric outlet obstruction. Stomach cannot empty → forceful expulsion of contents. Because obstruction is proximal to bile entry, emesis is nonbilious. Repeated HCl loss → hypochloremic, hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis.
Why Features Make Sense
  • Projectile nonbilious vomiting → gastric obstruction, no bile = proximal to duodenum
  • "Hungry vomiter" → stomach never truly empties; hunger signal intact
  • Olive mass RUQ → hypertrophied pyloric muscle = palpable
  • Peristaltic waves left→right → stomach fights the obstruction visibly
  • Dehydration signs → fluid can't be retained or absorbed
  • Low Cl⁻, low K⁺, high HCO₃⁻ → HCl lost in vomitus; kidney retains Na⁺/HCO₃⁻
At Risk First-born males · Family history · Macrolide use <2 wks (erythromycin, azithromycin) · ~2/1,000 live births
Subjective / ROS Forceful projectile nonbilious vomiting at 2–8 wks · Hunger immediately after emesis · Weight loss · Decreased wet diapers · Sunken fontanelle, dry mouth
Objective Exam Palpable "olive" mass in RUQ · Visible left→right peristaltic waves before vomit · Signs of dehydration (sunken fontanelle, dry mucous membranes)
Diagnostics Ultrasound (gold std — measures pylorus thickness) · Upper GI series if US inconclusive · Labs: ↓Cl⁻, ↓K⁺, ↑HCO₃⁻; electrolyte panel
DDx GERD · Gastroenteritis · Intestinal obstruction
Management
⚠ Correct fluids/electrolytes FIRST, then surgery
Pyloromyotomy (Ramstedt procedure) · Refer to pediatric surgeon · Resume feeds shortly after surgery; reflux common but normal post-op
Umbilical Hernia
Structural Anomaly · Usually Benign / Self-Resolving
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Failure of abdominal fascia to close → umbilical ring remains open → abdominal contents protrude. Most close spontaneously by age 5 as fascia matures.
Why Features Make Sense
  • Enlarges with crying/straining → increased intra-abdominal pressure pushes contents through ring
  • Soft, reducible, nontender → no incarceration; contents can return freely
At Risk Defect >1.5 cm · Age >5 years · Prematurity
Subjective / Objective Fluctuating soft mass at umbilicus, worse with crying/straining · Reducible without difficulty · No routine labs/imaging needed
Management
  • Observation — most resolve by age 5
  • Refer surgeon if: persistent past age 5, defect >1.5 cm, or symptomatic
Emergency referral if incarcerated: hard, painful, discolored, non-reducible mass + vomiting
Do NOT tape coins or occlusive dressings → infection risk, no benefit
Intussusception
Surgical Emergency · Leading Cause of Pediatric Intestinal Obstruction
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Proximal intestine "telescopes" into distal segment → obstruction, vascular compromise → ischemia → mucosal sloughing. Most common at ileocecal junction. 75% idiopathic; 25% have lead point (Meckel, lymphoma, polyp).
Why Features Make Sense
  • Episodic pain (legs drawn in) → peristalsis pushes against the telescoped segment in waves (10–20 min cycles)
  • Pain-free intervals → peristalsis relaxes temporarily
  • "Currant jelly" stools → ischemic mucosa bleeds + sloughs = blood + mucus
  • Vomiting nonbilious → bilious → worsening obstruction
  • Sausage mass RUQ → telescoped segment is palpable
  • Dance's sign (empty RLQ) → cecum has migrated from normal position
  • Lethargy → toxin absorption from ischemic bowel
At Risk Age 3 months–3 years (peak 5–9 months) · Males · Recent viral illness (adenovirus, rotavirus) · Rotavirus vaccine (1–5/100,000 — benefits outweigh risk)
Diagnostics Ultrasound → "target sign" (preferred) · Plain films → lack of air in rectum · Air/contrast enema = both diagnostic AND therapeutic
Management
Emergent referral to pediatric radiologist/surgeon
Air contrast enema under fluoroscopy (~75–90% success) · Surgery if enema fails or peritonitis present · IV fluids if dehydrated · Observe 12–24 hrs post-reduction · 10% recurrence rate
Hirschsprung Disease
Congenital Aganglionic Megacolon · Structural / Surgical
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Congenital absence of ganglion cells in colon (typically rectosigmoid) → loss of peristalsis → functional obstruction. Neural crest cells fail to migrate to distal bowel during fetal development. Aganglionic segment cannot relax → stool accumulates proximally.
Why Features Make Sense
  • Failure to pass meconium <48 hrs → distal bowel can't relax/propel stool
  • Abdominal distention → stool/gas accumulates behind obstruction
  • Explosive stool on rectal exam → examiner's finger bypasses aganglionic segment → releases retained stool
  • Tight anal sphincter → tonic contraction without neural inhibition
  • Ribbon/pebble stools (older child) → stool squeezed through narrow aganglionic segment
At Risk Males (4:1) · Down syndrome (Trisomy 21) · Family history · 1–2/10,000 births
Diagnostics Suction rectal biopsy (gold standard — shows absent ganglion cells) · Plain films: dilated loops + no rectal air · Barium enema: transition zone
DDx Functional constipation · Meconium ileus (cystic fibrosis) · Malrotation · Meconium plug syndrome
Management
Emergent referral to pediatric GI + surgeon
Surgical resection of aganglionic segment · Post-op: constipation and encopresis common — needs long-term management
Watch for: Enterocolitis (fever, distention, pain) — refer immediately

Acute GI Disorders

Appendicitis
Acute Surgical Emergency · Ages 10–19 Most Common
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Obstruction of appendix lumen (fecalith, lymphoid tissue, neoplasm) → bacterial overgrowth → inflammation → ischemia → perforation (within days). Located at base of cecum in RLQ.
Why Features Make Sense
  • Periumbilical → RLQ pain migration → visceral pain (vague) becomes somatic (localized) as peritoneum inflamed
  • Anorexia → hallmark; inflammation suppresses appetite via cytokines
  • Low-grade fever → infection; high-grade = perforation
  • Guarding/rebound at McBurney's → peritoneal irritation at appendix site
  • Rovsing sign → LLQ palpation increases RLQ pressure
  • Psoas/obturator sign → inflamed appendix adjacent to these muscles
  • Pain resolution ≠ good → may signal perforation (pressure released)
Diagnostics Ultrasound (first-line, no radiation) → thickened appendix, free fluid · CT if US inconclusive · CBC: ↑WBC + neutrophils · ↑CRP · Urine pregnancy (females before CT/surgery)

Pediatric Appendicitis Score (PAS):
2 pts: Pain migration to RLQ / tenderness with cough, hop, or percussion
1 pt each: Anorexia · Nausea/vomiting · Tenderness on palpation · Fever >100.4°F · WBC >10k · Left shift
≤3 = low risk (monitor) · 4–7 = indeterminate (serial exam + imaging) · ≥7 = emergent referral
Management
Emergent referral to pediatric ED or surgeon
Appendectomy + antibiotics + pain control · Perforation → longer hospitalization, multiple procedures
Acute Gastroenteritis
Self-Limiting · Primary Concern = Dehydration
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Viral, bacterial, or parasitic pathogens damage intestinal mucosa → increased intestinal fluid, impaired absorption → osmotic/secretory diarrhea. Leading agents: rotavirus, norovirus, adenovirus.
Why Features Make Sense
  • Watery diarrhea → damaged mucosa cannot absorb; increased secretion
  • Vomiting resolves before diarrhea → upper GI recovers faster
  • Fever → inflammatory response to pathogen
  • Crampy pain + hyperactive bowel sounds → bowel hypermotility
Diagnostics Clinical diagnosis · Stool culture if bloody, high fever, or travel · CBC: elevated neutrophils = bacterial · Viral antigen tests if severe
Management ORS = gold standard for mild–moderate dehydration
· 50–100 mL/kg over 4 hrs in small frequent volumes (5–15 mL q2–5 min)
· Resume regular diet immediately after rehydration
· NO antidiarrheals (↑ viral load) · NO antibiotics (viral) · NO plain water, juice, or soda
Emergent referral if severe dehydration, failed ORS, or signs of sepsis
Dehydration Classification & Management
Complication of GI Losses · Isotonic Most Common
Severity% Volume LossSignsManagement
Mild 3–5% Slightly decreased fluids, mild irritability, thirst or fluid refusal ORS 50–100 mL/kg over 4 hrs · Continue feeds
Moderate 6–9% Dry mucous membranes · Sunken fontanelle · ↓ skin turgor · Tachycardia · Tachypnea · Sunken eyes ORS (same) · Consider antiemetics to improve tolerance
Severe ≥10% Above + lethargy/obtundation · ↓BP · Cool extremities · Prolonged cap refill · No tears · Minimal urine Emergency referral for IV isotonic NS 20 mL/kg bolus
Types: Isotonic (most common, equal Na/H₂O loss) · Hypertonic (↑Na) · Hypotonic (↓Na — risk if rehydrated with free water in infants). Never use plain water, juice, or soda for rehydration.

Chronic GI Disorders

Constipation / Encopresis
Functional (most common) vs. Organic
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Functional: Low fiber/fluid, behavioral avoidance → hard stool retained → harder to pass → pain → more avoidance (vicious cycle). Encopresis: liquid stool seeps around impacted mass → soiling undergarments. Organic: Hirschsprung, hypothyroidism, celiac, spinal anomalies.
Why Features Make Sense
  • Encopresis is NOT behavioral → overflow from impaction; child cannot control it
  • Urinary incontinence → distended rectum compresses bladder
  • Abdominal pain → distended bowel + cramping from peristalsis against hard stool
Diagnosis ≥2 of the following for ≥1 month: ≤2 defecations/wk · Hard/painful BM · History of stool retention · Large fecal mass in rectum · Large-diameter stools
Management 1. Disimpact first (if impacted) with PEG
2. Maintenance: PEG (MiraLax) daily — tasteless/odorless, mix in water/juice
<4 mo 1–2 oz diluted prune juice · >4 mo lactulose 1mg/kg/day
3. Behavior: Timed toilet sitting 15 min twice daily (after meals) · Sticker chart
Alarm signs → refer: Fever, vomiting, rectal bleeding, abdominal distention, failure to pass meconium
GERD
Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease · GER vs GERD
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology GER = normal physiologic reflux occurring several times daily. GERD = when LES pressure < gastric pressure → chronic acid exposure → symptoms/mucosal injury. Affects 1.8–8.2% of children; higher in premature, obese, neuromuscular disorders.
Why Features Make Sense
  • Regurgitation/heartburn → acid in esophagus (no squamous lining protection)
  • Hoarseness, cough, wheeze → acid microaspiration irritates airway
  • Poor weight gain (infants) → can't retain enough calories
  • Irritability → pain from esophageal acid exposure
At Risk Prematurity · Obesity · Neuromuscular/developmental disorders · Pulmonary disease · Spastic CP
Management Step 1 (lifestyle first):
· Infants: upright after feeds, slower-flowing nipple, smaller/frequent feeds
· Older: ↓weight, avoid caffeine/chocolate/spicy/fat, elevate HOB, no eating before bed

Step 2 (pharmacologic):
· H2 blockers (famotidine) → short-term
· PPIs (omeprazole) → moderate-severe or persistent
Refer GI if: PPI fails · Bilious/bloody vomiting · Weight loss · Nocturnal pain · Fever — these are NOT GERD symptoms
Functional Abdominal Pain (FAP-NOS) & IBS
Functional GI Disorders · Diagnosed via Rome IV Criteria
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology FAP-NOS: Central dysregulation of pain + altered GI motility (↓ gastric motility, ↓ antral contractions). Brain-gut axis dysfunction — pain may originate centrally.

IBS: Brain-gut axis disorder with visceral hypersensitivity + gut inflammation + dysmotility. Pain processed differently than in healthy individuals.
Why Features Make Sense
  • No organic findings → pain is real but centrally/functionally mediated
  • Stress triggers pain → gut-brain axis: stress → ↑ visceral sensitivity
  • IBS: pain with stool changes → gut dysmotility affects both pain and stool form
  • School avoidance → pain is functionally limiting
Rome IV Criteria FAP-NOS: Episodic/continuous pain ≥4×/month for ≥2 months · Not solely with physiologic events · Insufficient criteria for IBS/dyspepsia/migraine

IBS: Recurrent pain ≥4 days/month for ≥2 months + at least one of: pain related to defecation · change in stool frequency · change in stool consistency. Pain in IBS does NOT fully resolve even when constipation is treated (if it does = functional constipation)
Risk Factors FAP: Bullying, school anxiety, divorce, abuse · IBS: Early trauma/surgery, anxiety/depression, family history IBD
Management FAP Maintain normal routines · CBT/hypnotherapy · SSRIs or amitriptyline (refractory) · Probiotics

IBS Low-FODMAP diet · Fiber + probiotics · CBT · SSRIs if severe

Alarm signs → organic workup: Weight loss · Nocturnal pain waking child · GI bleeding · Fever · Persistent RUQ/RLQ pain · Elevated inflammatory markers

Urinary Tract Infection (UTI) / Pyelonephritis
Most Common Cause: E. coli · Febrile UTI → Suspect Pyelonephritis
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Ascending bacterial infection from urethra → bladder (cystitis) → ureters → kidneys (pyelonephritis). E. coli = 80%+ of cases. Pyelonephritis → risk of renal scarring, especially with other bacteria. Girls at higher risk due to shorter urethra proximity to anus.
Why Features Make Sense
  • Dysuria, frequency, urgency → bladder/urethral mucosal irritation
  • Malodorous urine → bacterial metabolism products
  • Fever (high) + CVA tenderness → kidney involvement (pyelonephritis)
  • Young infants present with fever only → can't localize/verbalize symptoms
At Risk Girls <4 yrs · Uncircumcised boys <1 yr · Caucasian · Constipation · Voiding postponement · Poor hygiene · Labial adhesions · Previous UTI · Neurogenic bladder · VUR · Sexual activity
Diagnostics Urine culture = gold standard (2–3 days for result)
· Catheterization/suprapubic: >50,000 CFU/mL = positive
· Clean catch: >100,000 CFU/mL = positive
· UA: leukocyte esterase + · nitrites + · WBC/bacteria
· After 1st febrile UTI (2–24 mo): RBUS
· VCUG if RBUS shows abnormalities OR after 2nd UTI (2–24 mo)
Never perform VCUG during active infection (infected urine can cause temporary VUR or worse pyelonephritis)
Management · Cephalexin 50–100 mg/kg/day ÷ BID (low risk)
· Cefdinir 14 mg/kg/day daily (high risk)
· Cefixime or Cefuroxime alternatives
· Duration: 7–14 days (AAP)
· Review culture results; adjust if ESBL positive
· Education: Wipe front to back · Timed voiding q2h · Treat constipation · Avoid bladder irritants (citrus, caffeine, artificial sweeteners)
Refer: Infants <2 mo with febrile UTI → hospital for IV antibiotics · Recurrent UTIs → pediatric urology
Vesicoureteral Reflux (VUR)
Retrograde Urine Flow · Risk of Renal Scarring
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Incompetent ureterovesical junction valve → urine flows retrograde from bladder → ureters → renal collecting system. With cystitis, infected urine can ascend → pyelonephritis → irreversible renal scarring. Familial tendency: >30% sibling prevalence.
Why Features Make Sense
  • Recurrent febrile UTIs → infected urine has vehicle to reach kidneys via reflux
  • Often asymptomatic between UTIs → reflux doesn't cause pain unless infection present
Grading (VCUG) I: Retrograde to non-dilated ureter (not reaching pelvis)
II: Reaches renal pelvis, no dilation
III: Mild–moderate ureteral dilation, blunted fornices
IV: Dilated pelvis + calyces, tortuous ureter
V: Markedly dilated ureter + pelvis + calyces, severe tortuosity
Management Medical: Annual RBUS + VCUG · Antibiotic prophylaxis (all non–toilet-trained, bowel/bladder dysfunction, grades III–V):
· <2 mo: amoxicillin or cephalexin 25 mg/kg/day qHS
· >2 mo: TMP-SMX 2 mg/kg/day or nitrofurantoin 1–2 mg/kg/day qHS
· Timed voiding q2–3h · Treat bowel/bladder dysfunction

Surgical (grades III–V): Ureteral reimplantation · Endoscopic Deflux injection
Refer grades III–V to pediatric urology
Nocturnal Enuresis
Bed-Wetting · ~10% of 7-year-olds · Behavioral → Pharmacologic Step Approach
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Multifactorial: ↓ ADH secretion at night · Small bladder capacity · Detrusor overactivity · Heavy sleeper · Constipation (distended rectum compresses bladder). Primary (no dry periods ≥6 mo) vs. Secondary (new onset after dry period → rule out UTI, DM, stress).
At Risk Family history (strong) · ADHD · Constipation · Small bladder capacity · Heavy sleep · Meatal stenosis · UTI · Stress
Diagnostics UA (rule out UTI, glucosuria for DM) · Urine culture if abnormal UA · Plain abdominal film if constipation suspected
Management (Stepwise)
Medication is NEVER first-line
1st line (behavioral): Timed voiding q2h · No fluids 2 hrs before bed · Void before bed · Manage constipation · Bladder diary

2nd line: Bedwetting alarm (best for motivated families, >8 yrs, use 3–6 months consistently)

3rd line (pharmacologic):
· Desmopressin 0.2 mg PO 1 hr before bed (max 0.4 mg) — caution: hyponatremia risk with excess water
· Oxybutynin (anticholinergic) if detrusor overactivity — no post-void residual or constipation before use
Inguinal Hernia
Always Refer Surgically — Does NOT Resolve Spontaneously
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Failure of processus vaginalis to close → abdominal contents protrude into inguinal canal. In males: indirect hernia = testes migration pathway failed to close. In females: Nuck diverticulum fails to close. Right side more common. 1–5% term newborns; 9–11% preterm.
Why Features Make Sense
  • Bulge increases with straining/crying → increased intra-abdominal pressure pushes contents through patent canal
  • Reducible with gentle pressure → contents return freely to peritoneum
  • Incarceration pain/vomiting → bowel trapped + obstructed
At Risk Prematurity · Chronic lung disease · Connective tissue disorders (Ehlers-Danlos)
Management All inguinal hernias → Refer to pediatric surgeon/urologist for surgical repair
Emergent referral to ED if incarcerated: firm/irreducible bulge, discoloration, pain, nausea/vomiting, abdominal bloating
Labial Adhesions
Ages 3 mo – 6 yrs · Low Estrogen State
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Low estrogen in prepubertal state → thin, susceptible vulvar mucosa. Inflammation (poor hygiene, chemical/physical trauma, infection) → labia minora adhere together. Resolves spontaneously at puberty as estrogen rises.
Features Partial or complete closure of introitus · Urine dribbling · Erythema/swelling
Management · Mild: observation → resolves at puberty
· Symptomatic (blocks urine flow, UTIs): Estrogen cream 0.1% BID OR betamethasone 0.05% BID × 6–8 wks
· After separation: petroleum jelly several times daily to prevent recurrence
Recurrence is common if post-separation care not maintained
Refer to urology if no response at 8 weeks
Vulvovaginitis
Inflammation of Vulva + Vagina · Most Common Gynecologic Issue in Children
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Prepubertal vagina is alkaline and lacks lactobacilli protection (low estrogen) → susceptible to irritants, bacteria, yeast, parasites. Causes: poor hygiene, irritants (soaps, bubble baths), restrictive clothing, chronic masturbation, STI.
Features Pruritus · Dysuria · Vaginal discharge · Erythema · No routine lab testing needed for uncomplicated cases
Management · Topical anti-yeast + low-potency steroid cream BID (≤7 days for steroid)
· If allergic: topical/oral antihistamines
· If parasite: antiparasitic
· Education: Cotton underwear · No bubble baths/harsh soaps · Front-to-back wipe · Spread-leg voiding · Remove wet bathing suit promptly
Cryptorchidism
Undescended Testis · Refer if Not Descended by 6 Months Corrected Age
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Arrest of normal testicular descent from posterior abdomen → inguinal canal → scrotum (normally complete in 3rd trimester). Associated with ↓ fertility (especially bilateral) and ↑ risk of germ cell tumors. Up to 4% term boys; 45% preterm.
At Risk Prematurity · Low birth weight · Maternal DM · Maternal smoking · ↓ androgen production · Trisomy 21 · Klinefelter · Prune belly syndrome
Diagnostics Clinical diagnosis only · Scrotal ultrasound NOT recommended (not sensitive for testis position)
Key Exam Tip To differentiate undescended vs. retractile testis: manipulate testis to scrotum, hold several seconds to fatigue cremasteric muscle, release. If it stays = retractile. If it springs back = undescended.
Management · 34–43% spontaneously descend before 3 months
· If not descended by 6 months corrected age → Refer urology for orchidopexy
· Orchidopexy after puberty has fewer benefits on semen analysis — timing matters
· Teach monthly self-exam at puberty (↑ testicular cancer risk)
Testicular Torsion
⚠ UROLOGIC EMERGENCY — Window is 4–8 Hours
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Spermatic cord twists → interrupts vascular supply to testis → ischemia → infarction. Intravaginal (older boys — within tunica vaginalis) vs. Extravaginal (neonates). "Bell clapper deformity" = absent posterior scrotal wall attachment → testis swings freely → torsion.
Why Features Make Sense
  • Sudden severe testicular pain → acute ischemia
  • Wakes from sleep → cremasteric muscle contraction can trigger torsion
  • Absent cremasteric reflex → highly sensitive; spermatic cord twisted = reflex arc disrupted
  • High-riding horizontal testis → cord rotation pulls testis upward
  • Nausea/vomiting → autonomic response to severe pain
  • Always examine testes in males with abdominal pain → referred pain from torsion
Diagnostics Scrotal US with Doppler → absent blood flow to affected testis (normal in unaffected) · If intermittent torsion → US may be normal between episodes
Management
Immediate surgical detorsion by urologist
Salvage rate >90% if within 4–8 hours · Unlikely salvageable after 24 hours
vs. Epididymitis: pain is gradual onset, + Prehn sign (pain relief with scrotal elevation), normal cremasteric reflex, + blood flow on US
Phimosis & Paraphimosis
Paraphimosis = Urologic Emergency
DomainDetails
Phimosis Physiologic (primary): Natural adhesion of foreskin to glans in infants/young boys — normal, improves with age
Pathologic (secondary): Due to scarring, recurrent balanitis, or forced retraction
Rx: Low-potency topical corticosteroids BID × 6–8 wks · Circumcision if persistent
Paraphimosis Foreskin retracted and not reduced → trapped proximal to glans → edema → constrictive band → glanular gangrene risk
Medical emergency → refer to ED immediately for emergent foreskin reduction
Dysmenorrhea
Primary (no pathology) vs. Secondary (organic cause)
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Primary: Endometrial prostaglandin production/release during menstrual phase → intense uterine contractions → ↓ uterine blood flow → ischemia → pain. No pelvic pathology. Begins 6–12 months after menarche.

Secondary: Identifiable organic pathology causing pain (endometriosis, fibroids, IBS, interstitial cystitis, PID, STI, hernia). Typically begins in early 20s–30s.
Why Features Make Sense
  • Crampy pain at onset of menses, lasting 8–72 hrs → maximal prostaglandin release at start of menstruation
  • Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea → prostaglandins act on GI smooth muscle too
  • Dizziness → vasovagal response to pain + uterine ischemia
  • NSAIDs work well → directly inhibit prostaglandin synthesis
Primary Risk Factors Early menarche <12 yrs · BMI <20 · Smoking · History of sexual abuse · Nulliparity · Heavy/prolonged flow · Depression · Family history
Diagnostics Primary: Clinical diagnosis — no testing needed
Secondary: US, laparoscopy, cultures, hCG, UA depending on suspected cause
Management 1st line — NSAIDs: Most effective when started at onset of menses and taken around the clock 48–72 hrs
· Ibuprofen 400–800 mg PO q6h
· Naproxen 500 mg then 250 mg q6–8h
· Diclofenac 50 mg q8h

Hormonal (especially if contraception desired):
· Combined OCPs → suppress ovulation → ↓ prostaglandins
· DMPA (Depo) → endometrial atrophy → ↓ flow + ↓ prostaglandins
· LNG-IUS → 99% effective contraception + ↓ flow
· Nexplanon (progestin implant)

Adjuncts: Exercise · Heat compresses · Yoga/relaxation · Acupuncture · Vitamin E 200–400 IU BID (start 2 days before menses)
Amenorrhea
Primary vs. Secondary · Always Rule Out Pregnancy First
DomainDetails
Definitions Primary: No menses by age 15 with breast development OR 3 years after thelarche
Secondary: Absence ≥3 cycles (regular cycler) OR ≥6 months (irregular cycler)
Pathophysiology Disruption of HPO axis (hypothalamic-pituitary-ovarian) → no ovulation → no menstruation. Common causes: functional hypothalamic amenorrhea (stress, underweight, exercise), PCOS, thyroid disease, prolactinoma, structural anomalies, premature ovarian insufficiency.
At Risk Eating disorders (anorexia, bulimia) · Extreme athletes (gymnasts, runners, ballet dancers) · Significant weight loss · Stress · PCOS (most common cause of secondary)
Risks of Untreated Infertility · Osteoporosis · Scoliosis · Stress fractures · ↑ risk breast and reproductive cancers
Diagnostics hCG first (always rule out pregnancy)
· TSH + free T4 (thyroid)
· FSH, LH, estradiol (pituitary/ovarian function)
· Prolactin (prolactinoma)
· Total testosterone (PCOS)
· Pelvic ultrasound (structural)
· Assess Tanner stage
Management Secondary (hCG negative):
· Progestin challenge: Provera 10 mg PO × 10 days OR Prometrium 400 mg qHS × 10 days
· Expect withdrawal bleeding 2–7 days after completion
· No bleeding → refer gynecology

· Eating disorder → mental health referral
· Athlete/underweight → nutritional counseling, balanced exercise/rest
· Structural anomaly → refer gynecology
· Positive hCG → refer OB/midwife

Packaging Deformities

Result of restricted intrauterine space → abnormal position of limbs.

Congenital Torticollis
Why it happens: Intrauterine positioning → SCM muscle shortened/fibrous → head tilt toward affected side, rotation away

Features: Head tilt + rotation, limited passive ROM, palpable painless SCM mass

Rx: Home stretching exercises / PT · Surgical if unresolved >6 mo

Referral: PT if home stretching fails at 4 wks · Ortho if >6 mo unresolved
DDH — Hip Dysplasia
Why it happens: Abnormal hip joint development → dislocation/subluxation. More common in breech, female, first-born.

Exam: + Galeazzi (LLD) · + Barlow (dislocates) · + Ortolani (reduces clunk) · + Trendelenburg (walking age) · Uneven skin folds

Dx: Hip ultrasound (≤4–6 wks) → X-ray after 6 mo

Rx: <6 mo → Pavlik harness · >6 mo → closed/open reduction + casting · All → refer pediatric ortho
Clubfoot (Talipes Equinovarus)
Why it happens: Intrauterine positioning → foot deformity: midfoot cavus + forefoot adductus + hindfoot varus + hindfoot equinus

Features: Calf atrophy affected side · Medial/posterior creases · Shorter wider foot

Rx: Ponsetti method (serial casting) → percutaneous TAL → braces 23/24 hrs × 3 mo → nighttime bracing to age 3

Referral: Pediatric ortho immediately at diagnosis
Metatarsus Adductus
Why it happens: Intrauterine positioning → forefoot adduction, hindfoot neutral (≠ clubfoot)

Features: "Bean-shaped" foot · C-shaped lateral border · Deep medial crease · Wide space 1st/2nd toes

Rx: 90% self-resolve · Home stretching · Serial casting if no improvement · Spontaneous resolution by age 4

Referral: Ortho if no improvement after 4–6 wks stretching

Hip Disorders

SCFE — Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis
Adolescent Hip Emergency · Non-Weight-Bearing Until Surgery
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Displacement of femoral head (epiphysis) from femoral neck through the physis (growth plate) → disrupts blood supply → risk of avascular necrosis. Most common in 11–16 year olds. Stable (can bear weight) vs. Unstable (cannot).
Why Features Make Sense
  • Obese males, African American/Polynesian → increased mechanical forces on growth plate
  • Knee/thigh pain (not hip) → obturator nerve refers pain distally — ALWAYS check hips for knee pain
  • Externally rotated leg when walking → femoral head has slipped posteriorly; ER is position of least pain
  • ↓ Internal rotation + abduction → structural malalignment of femoral head
At Risk Obesity · Male · African American/Polynesian · Ages 11–16 · Endocrinopathies (hypothyroidism, renal failure) · Prior radiation/chemo
Diagnostics AP + lateral X-ray ("scoop of ice cream falling off cone") · Widened growth plate · Endocrine workup if suspected underlying disorder
Management
Strict non-weight-bearing immediately · Surgical fixation within 24 hrs of diagnosis
Long-term: ↑ risk degenerative joint disease even with fixation · May need total hip replacement later in life
Legg-Calvé-Perthes Disease (LCPD)
Avascular Necrosis of Femoral Head · Ages 4–9 · Years-Long Process
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Deficient blood flow to femoral head → avascular necrosis. 4 stages: Necrosis → Fragmentation → Reossification → Healing. Bilateral in 10%. Most common ages 4–9, male, Caucasian.
Why Features Make Sense
  • Gradual limp (not acute) → ischemia is progressive, not sudden like torsion
  • Pain relieved by rest → decreased weight-bearing reduces ischemic stress
  • Thigh/knee pain (not hip) → referred via obturator nerve; ALWAYS check hip
  • Trendelenburg gait → weak abductors from hip pathology
Diagnostics AP + frog-leg X-rays (flattened femoral head depending on stage)
Management NSAIDs · ROM maintenance · Activity restriction · Bracing/casting · Surgery if >6 yrs + severe
Refer to pediatric ortho + PT · Families: process takes years to resolve

Spinal & Lower Extremity

Adolescent Idiopathic Scoliosis
Who: Female, premenarchal, family history, peak at ~age 11

What: 3D >10° spinal curve, cause unknown, no back pain (pain → not AIS)

Exam: Adams forward bend test · Scoliometer (ATR ≥7°) · Standing X-rays (Cobb angle >10° confirms)

Rx by curve:
10–20° → Observation q4–6 mo
25–40° (immature) → Brace until mature
>40–50° → Surgical fusion

Alarm: Bowel/bladder changes → immediate eval
Genu Varum / Genu Valgum
Normal progression: Bowed (varum) → neutral (age 3) → knock-knee (valgum) → slight valgus by age 7

Varum Normal <3 yrs · Pathologic if worsens after 2, early walker, overweight → consider Blount disease

Valgum Normal to age 7 · No treatment before 7 · Refer ortho if worsens or persists

No braces/treatment for physiologic forms
In-Toeing
Causes: Internal tibial torsion (6–12 mo) · Femoral anteversion (peaks age 5, resolves by 8)

Features: Pigeon-toe gait · "W" sitting (femoral anteversion) · May trip more than peers

Rx: Observation and reassurance — both self-limiting
No braces — not effective
Refer ortho if not resolved by age 8
Overuse Syndromes
Osgood-Schlatter: Pain/swelling at tibial tubercle · Adolescent athletes
Sever's: Heel pain with jumping/running · + calcaneal squeeze test
Shin splints: Tibia pain with repetitive running
Little League elbow: Medial elbow pain, ↓ ROM, locking
Patellofemoral pain: Anterior knee pain

All Rx: RICE · NSAIDs · Relative rest · Gradual return · PT if not resolving

Neuromuscular Disorders

Cerebral Palsy (CP)
Permanent, Static, Nonprogressive Brain Injury · Multidisciplinary Care
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Brain injury during fetal or infant development → permanent motor dysfunction. Causes: hypoxic-ischemic event, stroke, infection, prematurity, brain malformation, trauma. Although brain injury is static, clinical presentation changes as child grows.
Classification By movement: Spastic (↑ tone) · Athetoid (slow involuntary movements) · Ataxic (↓ coordination) · Mixed
By distribution: Hemiplegic (one side) · Diplegic (both LE) · Triplegic (both LE + one UE) · Quadriplegic (all 4)
Features (UMN pattern) Spasticity · Hyperreflexia · Persistent primitive reflexes · Joint contractures · Scoliosis · Hip subluxation · Cognitive disability (not universal) · Seizures · Neurogenic bladder
Diagnostics Diagnosis of exclusion · Brain MRI (guides diagnosis, identifies injury location)
Management PT/OT/ST (early intervention) · Botulinum toxin / baclofen / diazepam for spasticity (specialist prescribes) · Nutrition optimization · GERD management · Multidisciplinary clinic
Important: Motor impairment ≠ cognitive impairment. Include child in all conversations. Treat the same as other children.
Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy (DMD)
X-Linked Recessive · Progressive Degeneration · Boys Only
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Mutation in dystrophin gene (Xp21) → absent dystrophin protein → progressive muscle fiber degeneration. Begins with proximal muscles, progresses distally. Calf pseudohypertrophy = muscle replaced by fat/fibrous tissue (looks big but is weak). Death by ~age 30 (cardiac/respiratory).
Why Features Make Sense
  • Gower's sign → proximal hip/thigh weakness; must use arms to climb up own body
  • Waddling gait / toe walking → proximal weakness; compensatory posturing
  • Calf pseudohypertrophy → degenerated muscle replaced by fat/fibrous tissue
  • CK >10× normal → ongoing muscle breakdown releases CK into blood
  • Appears normal early, then clumsier → initial compensation, then decompensation
Diagnostics CK >10× normal · Genetic testing (dystrophin gene) · Muscle biopsy (absent dystrophin)
Management No cure · Corticosteroids / deflazacort (controversial) · Bracing · Tendon releases · Spinal fusion for scoliosis
Multidisciplinary: Neurology · Cardiology · Pulmonology · Ortho · PT/OT/ST
Annual influenza vaccine (for child + household)
Wheelchair by early adolescence · Genetic counseling for mother re: carrier status

Chromosomal Disorders

Down Syndrome (Trisomy 21)
Most Common Genetic Cause of Intellectual Disability · 1/800 Newborns
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Extra copy of chromosome 21 → extra genetic material → cognitive impairment, hypotonia, characteristic features. 95% = trisomy 21 (nondisjunction during meiosis). 3–4% = translocation (familial). 1–2% = mosaicism. Risk ↑ with maternal age (homologous chromosomes have been paired longer → harder to separate → nondisjunction).
Why Features Make Sense
  • Hypotonia → excess chromosome 21 disrupts neuromuscular development
  • Intellectual disability → extra genetic material → altered brain development
  • Protruding tongue → macroglossia + hypotonia of tongue muscles
  • Hearing/vision problems (common) → extra chromosome alters cochlear and ocular development
  • ↑ Leukemia risk → trisomy 21 disrupts hematopoietic cell regulation
  • Congenital heart defects (50%) → cardiac morphogenesis genes on chr 21
Features Up-slanting palpebral fissures · Epicanthal folds · Brachycephaly · Depressed nasal bridge · Small low-set ears · Protruding tongue · Single palmar crease · Sandal gap · Hypotonia · Heart murmur
Diagnostics Karyotype (NOT CMA — can't detect balanced rearrangements) · TSH at birth, 6 mo, 12 mo, then annually · ECHO/ECG at birth
Management Cardiology (ECHO at birth) · Audiology (birth, 6 mo, 12 mo, annually) · Ophthalmology (birth, annually 1–5 yrs) · Annual TSH · Cervical X-ray for atlantoaxial instability (sports) · Celiac screening annually from age 1 · Developmental assessment · Early intervention PT/OT/ST
Alert: Neck pain, gait changes, bowel/bladder changes → atlantoaxial instability → refer ortho/neurosurgery urgently
Turner Syndrome (45, X)
Only Females · 1/2,000–2,500 Live Births · Cardiac = Leading Cause of Death
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Partial or complete monosomy X → multiple system effects. Most commonly sporadic. Short stature due to SHOX gene haploinsufficiency. Ovarian failure → estrogen deficiency → no puberty, infertility. Cardiac malformations (bicuspid aortic valve, coarctation). Variable due to mosaicism.
Why Features Make Sense
  • Short stature (most common) → SHOX gene on X chromosome → haploinsufficiency → impaired bone growth
  • Webbed neck / lymphedema → lymphatic drainage impaired prenatally
  • Primary amenorrhea / infertility → absent/dysgenetic ovaries → no estrogen → no puberty
  • Cardiac disease = #1 mortality → bicuspid aortic valve → aortic dissection
Features Short stature · Webbed neck · Down-slanted palpebral fissures · Low-set ears · Lymphedema of hands/feet · Broad shield chest · Pectus excavatum · Scoliosis · Delayed puberty · Hearing loss
Diagnostics Karyotype (45,X or 45,X/46,XX) · SRY testing if Y material suspected · Renal US at diagnosis · ECHO + MRI · Annual TSH · Annual LFTs after age 10 · Celiac screening
Management GH therapy (start age 4–6) · Estrogen/progestin for puberty induction · Annual cardiac surveillance · Audiology · Ophthalmology · Endocrinology · Orthopedics (scoliosis, osteoporosis) · Dermatology (nevi changes)
Most women with TS lead independent lives · Most are infertile
Klinefelter Syndrome (47, XXY)
Only Males · 1/650 Males · Often Undiagnosed
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Extra X chromosome → additional gene dosage → impacts physical and cognitive development. Nondisjunction error in early embryogenesis. Extra X genes → Leydig cell dysfunction → ↓ testosterone → hypogonadism, infertility, gynecomastia. Most have mild features and are never diagnosed.
Why Features Make Sense
  • Tall stature → extra X chromosome alters growth hormone axis
  • Gynecomastia → ↓ testosterone → relative estrogen excess
  • Small testes after puberty → hyalinization of seminiferous tubules → infertility
  • Learning/language difficulties → extra X affects neurodevelopment
Features Tall stature · Gynecomastia · Small testes · Sparse body hair · Clinodactyly · Pes planus · Hypotonia · Developmental delay · Dyslexia · ADHD
Diagnostics Karyotype (47,XXY) · Sex hormone levels · Testicular ultrasound
Management Testosterone replacement therapy (TRT) → puberty induction and maintenance · Developmental/learning support · Annual assessment: growth, BP, testes, gynecomastia · DEXA scan · Monitor metabolic syndrome, T2DM · Refer genetics + endocrinology

Mendelian & Other Syndromes

Marfan Syndrome
Cause: FBN1 gene mutation → abnormal fibrillin → connective tissue weakness
Inheritance: Autosomal dominant (50% each pregnancy)

Features: Tall/thin · Arachnodactyly (+ wrist/thumb sign) · Pectus deformity · Scoliosis · Ectopia lentis · Myopia · Aortic root dilation · Mitral valve prolapse

Dx: Clinical (Ghent criteria) + FBN1 molecular testing + Annual ECHO

Rx: Annual ECHO · Beta-blockers/ARBs · No contact sports · No stimulants (caffeine, decongestants)
Avoid fluoroquinolones → ↑ aortic aneurysm/dissection risk
Neurofibromatosis Type 1 (NF1)
Cause: NF1 gene mutation (tumor suppressor) → neurofibromas, CNS tumors
Inheritance: Autosomal dominant · 50% de novo

Diagnosis (2+ of): ≥6 café-au-lait macules (>5mm prepubertal) · ≥2 neurofibromas or 1 plexiform · Axillary/inguinal freckling · Optic glioma · ≥2 Lisch nodules · Sphenoid dysplasia/tibial pseudarthrosis · 1st-degree relative with NF1

Rx: Annual monitoring (growth, BP, eyes, neuro, skin) · MRI for new neurologic symptoms
Café-au-lait count does NOT predict severity
Fragile X Syndrome
Cause: CGG repeat expansion (>200) in FMR1 gene on X chromosome → silenced gene → absent FMRP protein → impaired neuronal function
Inheritance: X-linked; males more severely affected

Features: Intellectual disability (IQ 40–45) · ADHD · ASD · Long face · Large ears · Macro-orchidism (postpubertal males) · Joint laxity · Scoliosis · Seizures

Dx: Molecular genetic testing (CGG repeat count) + CMA for co-occurring microdeletions

Rx: Supportive (psychopharmacologic + therapies) · Lower doses for psychotropics · Avoid metoclopramide
Prader-Willi Syndrome
Cause: Loss of paternal genes in 15q11.2–q13 region (imprinting disorder): deletion (70%), maternal uniparental disomy (20–30%), imprinting defect (2.5%)
Most common genetic cause of obesity

Features: Neonatal hypotonia + poor feeding → hyperphagia in childhood → obesity · Short stature · Small hands/feet · Narrow forehead · Almond-shaped eyes · Strabismus (60–70%) · Hypogonadism · Cryptorchidism · Behavioral problems

Dx: DNA methylation testing (detects 99%)

Rx: GH therapy (started in infancy) · Strict dietary control (hyperphagia cannot be controlled by child alone) · Behavior management · Refer endocrinology, urology, ophthalmology, ortho
Food Allergy
IgE-Mediated Immune Response · Distinct from Food Intolerance
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Sensitization to food antigen → allergen-specific IgE antibodies produced → re-exposure → IgE binds mast cells → degranulation → histamine + mediator release → systemic symptoms. Big 8 allergens: milk, egg, peanut, tree nuts, shellfish, fish, wheat, soy.
Why Features Make Sense
  • Urticaria/angioedema → histamine + mast cell mediators → vascular permeability
  • Throat tightening, wheeze → smooth muscle contraction in airways
  • Hypotension → massive vasodilation in anaphylaxis
  • GI symptoms → IgE receptors in gut mucosa
At Risk Family history · Atopic dermatitis · Asthma · Non-Hispanic African American · Prior allergies · Dysbiosis
Diagnostics Oral food challenge (gold standard — double-blind placebo-controlled) · Skin prick test · Allergen-specific IgE (serum) · Elimination diet (mild-moderate)
Management Strict allergen avoidance · Label reading · Epinephrine auto-injector for all patients with anaphylaxis risk · Dietitian referral for nutritional adequacy
Most children outgrow milk, egg, soy, wheat allergy
Call 911 immediately for anaphylaxis: throat tightening, ↓BP, dyspnea, loss of consciousness
Celiac Disease
Autoimmune Gluten-Induced Enteropathy · Lifelong Gluten-Free Diet
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Gluten (wheat/barley/rye) → tissue transglutaminase deamidates gliadin → binds HLA-DQ2/DQ8 on APCs → CD4+ T-cell activation → Th1/Th2 response → pro-inflammatory cytokines → villous atrophy + crypt hyperplasia → malabsorption. 90% carry HLA-DQ2, 5–7% carry DQ8. Must have HLA-DQ2/DQ8 to develop CD, but most with these genes do NOT develop CD.
Why Features Make Sense
  • Diarrhea/malabsorption → villous atrophy = ↓ absorptive surface area
  • Short stature/weight loss → nutrient malabsorption impairs growth
  • Anemia → iron/folate malabsorption (proximal small bowel most affected)
  • Dermatitis herpetiformis → IgA deposits in skin from celiac autoimmune process
  • Mood/neurologic symptoms → nutritional deficiencies + gluten antibody effects
At Risk 1st-degree relative with CD · Type 1 DM · IgA deficiency · Autoimmune thyroiditis · Trisomy 21 · Turner syndrome · Williams syndrome
Diagnostics Must be eating gluten during testing (10g/day × 8 wks minimum)
· Total IgA + TTG-IgA (initial screening)
· If IgA deficient → TTG-IgG or DGP-IgG
· EMA-IgA (confirmatory, more specific)
· HLA-DQ2/DQ8 (negative = CD excluded, 99% NPV)
· Duodenal biopsy via endoscopy (gold standard for most) — refer to GI
· Repeat TTG-IgA every 3–6 mo until normal after diet change
Management Lifelong strict gluten-free diet (wheat, barley, rye eliminated)
Oats: most tolerate but contamination risk
Monitor: iron, folate, B12, Vitamin D, zinc, calcium
Annual: TSH, CBC, celiac antibodies
Pneumococcal immunization (hyposplenism risk)
Adherence hardest in adolescence — intentional breaking common. GI bleeding, persistent Ab → investigate non-adherence first
Pediatric Obesity
BMI ≥95th Percentile · Complex, Multifactorial · Staged Approach
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Positive energy balance (intake > expenditure) → excess adiposity. Primary: polygenic susceptibility + obesogenic environment. Secondary (rare): Cushing, hypothyroidism, GH deficiency, PCOS, genetic syndromes (Prader-Willi, Bardet-Biedl), medications (glucocorticoids, antipsychotics).
Classification Overweight: BMI 85–94th %ile · Obesity (I): ≥95th %ile
Severe (II): >120% of 95th or BMI ≥35 · Severe (III): >140% of 95th or BMI ≥40
Comorbidities T2DM · Hypertension · Dyslipidemia · NAFLD · OSA · PCOS · SCFE (hip pain in obese) · Blount disease · Pseudotumor cerebri · Depression/anxiety
Screening T2DM: if ≥10 yrs or puberty onset + BMI ≥85th + 1 risk factor → fasting glucose / A1c / OGTT
Lipids: universal screening ages 9–11 · Nonfasting profile → fasting if abnormal
NAFLD: ALT starting age 9–11 if obese
Management (Staged) Stage 1 (Prevention Plus): PCP-guided dietary + activity goals
Stage 2 (Structured Weight Mgmt): Dietitian involvement, more frequent visits
Stage 3 (Comprehensive): Weekly multidisciplinary team, intensive behavioral modification
Stage 4 (Tertiary): Medical diets, pharmacotherapy (orlistat ≥12 yrs, phentermine ≥16 yrs, metformin for T2DM), or bariatric surgery (BMI ≥35 + comorbidity or BMI ≥40, ≥10 yrs)
Metabolic Syndrome
Cluster of Cardiometabolic Risk Factors · Insulin Resistance at Core
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Insulin resistance → liver loses glucose regulation → hyperglycemia. Simultaneously, insulin still drives hepatic lipogenesis → ↑ free fatty acids + triglycerides → dyslipidemia + adipose deposition. No single pediatric diagnostic definition exists — focus on clustering of risk factors.
Components (Adult Criteria as Reference) Elevated waist circumference · TG ≥150 · HDL <40 (M) / <50 (F) · BP ≥130/85 · FBG ≥100
Key Features Acanthosis nigricans (↑ insulin → ↑ keratinocyte growth) · Central adiposity · Hypertension · Dyslipidemia · Hyperglycemia · PCOS in adolescent females
Management Primary: Lifestyle — ↓ caloric intake, ↑ physical activity (aerobic 60 min/day), ↓ screen time
Treat individual components (HTN, dyslipidemia, hyperglycemia) · Refer endocrinology for prediabetes/T2DM
Pediatric Malnutrition / Undernutrition
Illness-Related OR Environmental/Behavioral · Serial Anthropometrics Key
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Imbalance between nutrient requirements and intake → cumulative deficits of energy/protein/micronutrients → impaired growth, development, and function. Illness-related (hypermetabolism, malabsorption, poor intake from disease) OR non-illness-related (food insecurity, behavioral, poverty).
Why Features Make Sense
  • Poor linear growth + weight loss → inadequate substrate for anabolism
  • Muscle wasting → body catabolizes muscle protein for energy
  • Hair/nail changes, dry skin → protein/micronutrient deficiencies
  • Cognitive/developmental delays → brain requires adequate nutrition, especially in early childhood
Assessment Plot weight, height, BMI, head circumference (≤2 yrs) on CDC/WHO growth charts · Mid-upper arm circumference · Prealbumin + albumin (limited by inflammation) · CBC, iron studies, vitamin D, B12, folate
Management Mild–moderate: Outpatient — increase caloric density, dietitian, treat underlying cause
Severe: Hospitalization, multidisciplinary team, structured refeeding plan
Refeeding syndrome risk in severely malnourished: hypophosphatemia, hypokalemia, CHF, seizures, hemolysis in first 1–2 weeks of refeeding → start slowly, monitor electrolytes

Thyroid Disorders

Congenital Hypothyroidism
Newborn Screening Detected · Risk of Mental Retardation if Untreated
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Thyroid gland dysgenesis (85%) or dyshormonogenesis (15%) → inadequate thyroxine (T4) production → impaired metabolism, growth, and neurodevelopment. Permanent (TSH >40) vs. Transient (TSH 20–39, resolves ~age 3). T4 critical for brain myelination and maturation.
Why Features Make Sense
  • Prolonged jaundice → hypothyroidism slows hepatic bilirubin conjugation
  • Constipation, bradycardia → low thyroid slows all smooth muscle and cardiac activity
  • Macroglossia, large fontanels → impaired bone/tissue development
  • Lethargy, poor feeding → low metabolic rate = low energy
  • If untreated → mental retardation → brain requires T4 for myelination in first years
Diagnostics Newborn screen TSH >40 → confirm with serum TSH + free T4 → treat immediately. TSH 20–40: confirm serum, treat if confirmed. Thyroid scan optional (identifies dysgenesis vs. dyshormonogenesis).
Management Levothyroxine 10–15 mcg/kg/day · Crush tablet, mix with breast milk/formula/water
Follow-up: 1–2 wks after start → q2 wks until TSH normalizes → q1–2 mo until 6 mo → q3–4 mo until age 3 → q6–12 mo thereafter
Refer to pediatric endocrine within 1–2 weeks of life
Soy formula may interfere with levothyroxine absorption — notify provider
Acquired Hypothyroidism (Hashimoto's Thyroiditis)
Autoimmune · Most Common Cause in Children >2 Years
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Autoimmune destruction of thyroid → chronic inflammation → ↓ T4 production. Antithyroid antibodies (TPO-Ab, thyroglobulin-Ab) attack thyroid. Occurs in 1/1,250 children. Usually permanent, requires lifelong treatment.
Why Features Make Sense
  • Weight gain, fatigue → ↓ T4 → ↓ metabolism
  • Cold intolerance → ↓ thermogenesis
  • Constipation, bradycardia → slowed smooth muscle and cardiac function
  • Poor growth / declining height velocity → T4 required for GH axis
  • Pebbly/rubbery goiter → lymphocytic infiltration + inflammation of gland
At Risk Female · Adolescent · Down syndrome · Turner syndrome · T1DM · Celiac disease · Other autoimmune conditions · Family history
Diagnostics ↑ TSH + ↓ free T4 (overt) · Anti-TPO Ab or Anti-thyroglobulin Ab (+) = Hashimoto's confirmed
Mild obesity can cause mild TSH elevation with normal T4 — does NOT require treatment
Management Levothyroxine (dose weight/age dependent) · Refer endocrinology
TSH >50 → urgent endocrine referral · Rapid correction can cause heart failure
TSH 5–10 + no symptoms → repeat in 2–3 mo before referring
Hyperthyroidism (Graves Disease)
Autoimmune Thyroid-Stimulating Antibodies · Urgent Referral
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulins (TSIs) bind TSH receptors → unregulated T4 + T3 production → ↑ metabolism across all systems. Goiter from TSI-driven gland growth. Exophthalmos from separate autoimmune process (extraocular muscle inflammation).
Why Features Make Sense
  • Weight loss with ↑ appetite → ↑ metabolic rate burns more calories
  • Tachycardia, widened pulse pressure → ↑ cardiac output from thyroid excess
  • Heat intolerance, sweating → ↑ thermogenesis
  • Tremor, anxiety → excess catecholamine sensitivity
  • Exophthalmos → NOT from T4 excess — from autoimmune extraocular muscle inflammation
  • Goiter (firm) → TSI drives gland proliferation
At Risk Female (5:1) · >11 yrs · Family history autoimmune thyroid · Down syndrome · Turner syndrome
Diagnostics ↑ Free T4 + ↑ T3 + suppressed TSH · TSI (detectable in ≥60% Graves) · TPO Ab · RAI uptake if antibodies negative
Management
Urgent referral to pediatric endocrine
Methimazole 0.25–1 mg/kg/day ÷ BID-TID × 18 mo minimum
Beta-blocker (atenolol 1–2 mg/kg/day) for symptom control while awaiting T4 normalization
RAI therapy: not for <10 yrs · Thyroidectomy: consider for <5 yrs
Pre-Rx: Check WBC, LFTs (baseline for monitoring agranulocytosis/hepatitis)
Thyroid storm: fever + altered mental status = medical emergency

Diabetes Mellitus

Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus (T1DM)
Autoimmune Beta Cell Destruction · Absolute Insulin Deficiency
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Genetic predisposition + environmental trigger (virus, dietary) → autoimmune attack on pancreatic beta cells → complete destruction → absolute insulin deficiency → uncontrolled hyperglycemia → DKA. Islet cell antibodies (GAD-65, IA-2, ZnT8, IAA) positive.
Why Features Make Sense
  • Polydipsia, polyuria → hyperglycemia → osmotic diuresis → fluid loss → thirst
  • Polyphagia → cells starving (no insulin to facilitate glucose entry)
  • Weight loss → body catabolizes fat/muscle for energy (no glucose utilization)
  • Fruity breath, Kussmaul respirations → ketone production from fat catabolism → metabolic acidosis → compensatory hyperventilation
  • Nocturia, enuresis → osmotic diuresis at night
Diagnostics Random glucose ≥200 + symptoms · Fasting glucose ≥126 · A1c ≥6.5% · Urine ketones · C-peptide (low) · Islet cell Ab panel
Management Insulin — life-sustaining
· Basal-bolus therapy (long-acting + rapid-acting) OR insulin pump
· BG monitoring ≥4× daily (pre-meals, bedtime, pre-exercise)
· A1c every 3 months
· DKA symptoms → ER immediately
· School DM management plan
If ketones present → start insulin within 6 hrs to avoid metabolic decompensation
Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus (T2DM)
Insulin Resistance + Relative Deficiency · Strongly Linked to Obesity
DomainDetails
Pathophysiology Insulin resistance (adiposity → inflammatory cytokines → block insulin signaling) + progressive beta cell failure → relative insulin deficiency → hyperglycemia. In children, decline in beta cell function is MORE RAPID than in adults. Nearly 80% with T2DM in children are obese.
Why Features Make Sense
  • Acanthosis nigricans → ↑ insulin → ↑ IGF-1 → keratinocyte proliferation → dark velvety skin patches at neck/axilla
  • Asymptomatic (50%) → gradual onset; enough insulin for partial function
  • Vulvovaginitis in adolescent girls → glucosuria creates environment for yeast overgrowth
Screening Age ≥10 or puberty (whichever first) + BMI ≥85th + ≥1 risk factor → fasting glucose / A1c / 2-hr OGTT · Repeat every 3 years minimum · More frequent if BMI worsening
At Risk Obesity · Family history T2DM · Native American, African American, Hispanic, Asian American, Pacific Islander · Gestational DM exposure · PCOS
Management 1st line: Lifestyle — weight reduction 7–10% (↑ insulin sensitivity) · ≥60 min activity/day · ↓ screen time · Dietitian
Pharmacologic: Metformin (first-line medication in children) · Start at 500 mg with meals
Screen for complications: nephropathy, neuropathy, retinopathy, NAFLD, OSA, CVD, PCOS
Refer: Endocrinology · Dietitian · Mental health (depression common)

Growth Disorders

Growth Hormone Deficiency
Why it happens: Genetic mutation or structural brain lesion → ↓ GH secretion from pituitary → ↓ IGF-1 → impaired linear growth

Features: Short stature · Delayed bone age · Immature facies · Frontal bossing · "Orange peel" fat distribution · Delayed puberty · Small penis

Dx: Low IGF-1 / IGFBP-3 · GH stimulation test (gold std, specialist orders) · Bone age X-ray

Rx: Recombinant GH (specialist prescribes) · q3–6 mo follow-up
Constitutional Delay (CDGP)
Why it happens: Normal variant — "late bloomer." HPO axis maturation delayed, not absent. Strong family history.

Features: Short stature · Low-normal growth velocity · Pubertal delay (girls: no thelarche by 12 / menarche by 16 · Boys: no testicular enlargement by 14) · Delayed bone age

Dx: Bone age delayed >1 yr from chronological age · Family history positive

Rx: Reassurance · Optimize nutrition · If psychosocial detriment: refer endocrine for hormone initiation (estradiol for girls, IM testosterone for boys)
Precocious Puberty
Why it happens: Early activation of HPO axis (central, CPP) OR peripheral estrogen/androgen excess. 90% girls / 60% boys idiopathic.

Definition: Girls: breast development <8 yrs · Boys: testicular enlargement <9 yrs

Risk: Short stature — sex hormones → advanced bone age → closes epiphyses early

Dx: Bone age X-ray (≥2 yrs advanced) · LH, FSH, estradiol or testosterone · Brain MRI (all boys + girls <6 yrs)

Rx: GnRH agonist (leuprolide) to halt puberty and preserve height · Refer endocrine
Adrenal Insufficiency
Why it happens: Primary: adrenal cortex can't produce cortisol/aldosterone (most common infant cause = CAH) · Central: pituitary ↓ ACTH

Features: Fatigue · N/V · Dizziness · Salt craving (primary) · Hyperpigmentation (chronic) · Hypoglycemia · Hypotension

Dx: Low morning cortisol + ↑ ACTH (primary) · Cosyntropin stimulation test (gold std)

Rx: Hydrocortisone 7–10 mg/m²/day TID · Fludrocortisone for mineralocorticoid deficiency · Stress dosing up to 50 mg/m² during illness
Adrenal crisis = emergency: ↓BP, dehydration, hypoglycemia, ↑K, ↓Na → IV hydrocortisone
