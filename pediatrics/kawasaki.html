Pathophysiology

The Story

Kawasaki disease is a medium-vessel vasculitis of unclear cause — the leading theory is that an infectious trigger sets off an abnormal immune response in a genetically susceptible child. It's not contagious, but outbreaks cluster seasonally (winter–spring peak) in a way that suggests an environmental trigger.

The inflammation has a particular appetite for coronary arteries. Left untreated, about 25% of children develop coronary artery aneurysms — and those aneurysms become a lifelong cardiac risk factor. This is why Kawasaki is the leading cause of acquired heart disease in children in developed countries, not rheumatic fever.

The child looks miserable. Fever goes on for days, unresponsive to antipyretics. The rash, red eyes, and cracked lips look like a viral exanthem at first — which is why Kawasaki gets missed. The trick is the combination: fever ≥5 days plus at least four of the CREAM findings, in a child who looks sicker than any typical viral illness.

Treat with IVIG within the first 10 days and aneurysm risk drops from ~25% to ~4%. Miss the window and the heart pays for decades. That time-sensitivity is why boards test this so aggressively.

Diagnosis

Diagnostic Criteria

Classic Kawasaki Disease
AHA / AAP 2017
Requires fever ≥5 days plus at least 4 of 5 principal clinical features. If the child has coronary artery abnormalities on echo, diagnosis can be made with fewer criteria.
C
Conjunctivitis
Bilateral, bulbar, non-exudative (no discharge), limbal sparing (white ring around iris)
R
Rash
Polymorphous — maculopapular, morbilliform, or scarlatiniform. Truncal. Not vesicular or bullous.
E
Extremity changes
Acute: erythema & swelling of palms/soles. Subacute (wk 2–3): periungual desquamation — classic peeling of fingers and toes
A
Adenopathy
Cervical lymph node ≥1.5 cm, usually unilateral. Least common feature — often missing in classic cases
M
Mucositis
Strawberry tongue, cracked & fissured red lips, diffuse oropharyngeal erythema. No discrete ulcers or Koplik spots
Incomplete Kawasaki
Fever ≥5 d but fewer than 4 criteria — common in infants <6 mo, who have highest aneurysm risk. Use supplemental labs (CRP, ESR, albumin, ALT, WBC, plts, UA) + echo. If labs suggestive → treat.
Clinical Course

The Three Phases

Phase 1
Acute
Days 1–11
Fever + all CREAM features appear. Child is irritable. Treatment window — IVIG here prevents aneurysms.
Phase 2
Subacute
Days 11–21
Fever resolves. Periungual desquamation, thrombocytosis (plts up to 1M), and coronary aneurysms appear. Highest sudden death risk.
Phase 3
Convalescent
Weeks 3–8
Clinical signs resolved. Labs normalize. Repeat echo at 2 wks and 6–8 wks to assess coronary status.
Workup

Diagnostic Ladder

Step 1 · Suspect
Clinical criteria
Fever ≥5 days + ≥4 CREAM features. Kawasaki is a clinical diagnosis — no single lab confirms it.
Step 2 · Support
Labs
↑ CRP, ESR, ALT · ↑ platelets (week 2) · normocytic anemia · sterile pyuria on UA · ↓ albumin. None are diagnostic but support the call.
Step 3 · Image
Baseline echocardiogram
At diagnosis. Assess coronary artery Z-scores, ventricular function, and valvular disease. Do not delay treatment waiting for echo.
Step 4 · Rule out
Consider mimics
Scarlet fever, measles, toxic shock, SJS, drug reaction, adenovirus, JIA. In 2020+: distinguish from MIS-C (post-COVID inflammatory syndrome).
Step 5 · Follow
Repeat echocardiography
At 2 weeks and 6–8 weeks post-treatment. More frequent if abnormalities found.
Management

Acute vs Long-term

🚨
Acute Treatment
  • 1stIVIG 2 g/kg as single infusion — ideally within 10 days of fever onset
  • PlusHigh-dose aspirin (80–100 mg/kg/day) until afebrile 48–72 h
  • ThenLow-dose aspirin (3–5 mg/kg/day) for 6–8 wks or until echo clears
  • If failSecond IVIG dose if fever persists 36 h post-infusion. Add steroids or infliximab if refractory
  • AdmitHospital-based care during IVIG. Delay live vaccines (MMR, varicella) 11 months after IVIG
🫀
Long-term Follow-up
  • No aneurysmLow-dose aspirin until echo normal. Discharge from cardio f/u.
  • SmallOngoing low-dose aspirin. Annual echo + cardio f/u.
  • MediumAspirin ± clopidogrel. Stress testing. Lifetime cardio f/u.
  • GiantAspirin + warfarin or LMWH. Frequent imaging. Consider CABG if ischemia.
  • FamilyCounsel on lifelong CV risk. Encourage activity, healthy diet, no smoking.
Board Pearls

✨ Don't Miss These

The 10-day window
IVIG within 10 days of fever onset drops aneurysm risk from ~25% to ~4%. This is the single most tested number.
Irritability is a huge clue
Kawasaki kids are disproportionately miserable — "inconsolable" is a classic stem. More irritable than any febrile viral illness should make them.
Aspirin exception
Aspirin is normally avoided in children (Reye syndrome risk), but in Kawasaki it's essential. Counsel family to watch for flu/varicella exposure; switch if needed.
Delay live vaccines
11 months after IVIG before MMR or varicella. IVIG blunts live-vaccine response. Inactivated vaccines are fine.
Infants <6 mo
Most likely to have incomplete Kawasaki AND highest aneurysm risk. Have a low threshold to treat a febrile infant with supportive labs and any CREAM features.
MIS-C mimic
MIS-C (multisystem inflammatory syndrome in children) post-COVID can mimic Kawasaki. Older kids, more GI, more shock, more myocardial dysfunction. Similar IVIG + steroid approach.
