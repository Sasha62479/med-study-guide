Inheritance Patterns — Quick Reference
Know the pattern before you learn the syndrome — it predicts who gets it, how severe, and recurrence risk.

Chromosomal · Trisomy 21
Down Syndrome
47,XX or XY +21
Most common chromosomal disorder and the most common genetic cause of intellectual disability.
🎨 Analogy

Imagine the genetic blueprint as a recipe book with 46 chapters. In Down syndrome, chapter 21 got photocopied an extra time and slipped back in — so the body builds everything from three copies of those instructions instead of two. The extra dosage affects development from the earliest weeks, shaping features across every organ system. The "recipe" still works — just with an extra portion of chromosome 21 flavoring everything.

Genetics
  • Trisomy 21 — nondisjunction (95%), most common
  • Translocation (4%) — chromosome 21 fused to another (often 14); NOT age-related; recurrence risk higher → karyotype parents
  • Mosaic (1%) — some cells trisomic; often milder phenotype
  • Risk ↑ with advanced maternal age: age 35 = 1:400; age 45 = 1:30
  • Screening: 1st trimester combined screen (PAPP-A, hCG, nuchal translucency), cell-free fetal DNA
  • Diagnosis: karyotype (gold standard)
Classic Features
  • Facial: flat facies, upslanting palpebral fissures, epicanthal folds, flat nasal bridge, small ears, macroglossia, Brushfield spots (iris)
  • Neck: short neck, excess nuchal skin
  • Hands: single palmar crease (simian crease), short 5th finger (clinodactyly), wide sandal gap between 1st–2nd toes
  • Tone: generalized hypotonia ⭐ — classic newborn finding
  • Intellectual disability (mild–moderate in most)
Associated Conditions — Must Screen
  • ❤️ Congenital heart disease (40–50%) — most common: AVSD (AV canal defect), VSD, ASD
  • 🦠 Duodenal atresia — "double bubble" sign on X-ray; bilious vomiting newborn
  • 🦠 Hirschsprung's disease (10×↑ risk)
  • 👁️ Cataracts, strabismus, refractive errors
  • 👂 Hearing loss (conductive + sensorineural)
  • 🦴 Atlantoaxial instability (C1–C2) — screen before sports/surgery
  • 🩸 ↑ Risk leukemia (ALL, AML), transient myeloproliferative disorder (TMD)
  • 🧠 Alzheimer's disease (virtually universal by age 40)
  • 🦋 Hypothyroidism (check TSH annually)
  • 😴 Obstructive sleep apnea
Surveillance Schedule
  • Newborn: echo, karyotype, hearing screen, ophthalmology, thyroid
  • Annually: TSH, hearing, vision, dental
  • Atlantoaxial instability: X-ray before sports or anesthesia requiring neck extension
  • Developmental monitoring: EI, PT, OT, speech
  • Hematology: CBC in newborn period (TMD)
  • Sleep study if OSA suspected
  • Life expectancy now >60 years with modern care
⚠️ Board Must-Know
Most common CHD in Down syndrome = AVSD (AV canal). Most common GI anomaly = duodenal atresia ("double bubble"). Translocation Down syndrome = check parental karyotype (higher recurrence risk).
Sex Chromosome · Monosomy X
Turner Syndrome
45,X (XO)
Females only — short stature, ovarian failure, and a webbed neck that tells the story at a glance.
🎨 Analogy

Think of the second X chromosome as a construction supervisor for female development. Without it, the ovaries — which start as primitive streaks in every embryo — never fully form and become streak gonads (fibrous tissue where ovaries should be). Meanwhile, the lymphatic system during fetal development backs up around the neck (cystic hygroma → webbed neck at birth) and the aorta may be narrowed (coarctation). The body is female in every other way — just missing the supervisor who was supposed to oversee the ovaries and a few other projects.

Genetics
  • 45,X — complete monosomy (most classic)
  • Mosaicism (45,X/46,XX) — milder phenotype
  • Isochromosome Xq — structural variant
  • Most common: nondisjunction of paternal sex chromosome
  • NOT associated with advanced maternal age
  • Incidence: 1:2,000–2,500 live female births
Classic Features
  • Short stature ⭐ — universal; below 3rd percentile without treatment
  • Webbed neck (pterygium colli) / low posterior hairline
  • Streak gonads → primary amenorrhea, infertility, no puberty
  • Widely-spaced nipples, shield chest
  • Cubitus valgus (increased carrying angle at elbow)
  • Lymphedema of hands and feet at birth (puffy hands/feet)
  • Low-set, posteriorly rotated ears
  • Multiple nevi
  • Normal intelligence — may have specific math/spatial difficulties
Associated Conditions
  • ❤️ Coarctation of the aorta — most common CHD ⭐
  • ❤️ Bicuspid aortic valve
  • 🩺 Hypertension (renal anomalies, aortic)
  • 🫘 Horseshoe kidney — most common renal anomaly
  • 👂 Recurrent otitis media, sensorineural hearing loss
  • 🦋 Autoimmune thyroiditis (Hashimoto's) — screen annually
  • 🩸 Celiac disease — screen
  • 🦴 Osteoporosis (from estrogen deficiency)
Management
  • Growth hormone — start early for short stature (even before puberty)
  • Estrogen replacement — start ~12–13 years for puberty induction; continue for bone density + cardiovascular health
  • Progestin added after estrogen priming (for uterine protection)
  • Fertility: possible with donor egg IVF
  • Echo at diagnosis; repeat every 5–10 years (aortic root surveillance)
  • Renal ultrasound at diagnosis
  • Annual: TSH, glucose, lipids, BP monitoring
  • Hearing screen, ophthalmology
Sex Chromosome · XXY
Klinefelter Syndrome
47,XXY
Males only — tall, small testes, infertility. Often not diagnosed until adolescence or adulthood.
Genetics & Epidemiology
  • 47,XXY — most common sex chromosome disorder in males
  • 1:500–1,000 male births
  • Variants: 48,XXXY; 49,XXXXY — more severe features
  • Risk ↑ with advanced maternal age
  • Often diagnosed incidentally on prenatal cell-free DNA
  • Most go undiagnosed until infertility workup in adulthood
Classic Features
  • Tall stature with long legs (eunuchoid proportions)
  • Small, firm testes (testicular atrophy/hyalinization) ⭐
  • Azoospermia → infertility (most common cause of male infertility from genetic cause)
  • Gynecomastia (↑ estrogen:testosterone ratio)
  • Sparse facial and body hair
  • Decreased muscle mass, ↑ adiposity
  • Language-based learning difficulties, reading delays
  • Mild intellectual or behavioral differences (not always)
Associated Conditions & Management
  • ↑ Risk: autoimmune diseases, type 2 DM, metabolic syndrome
  • ↑ Risk: breast cancer (20× vs. 46,XY males), mediastinal germ cell tumors
  • Osteoporosis from testosterone deficiency
  • Testosterone replacement starting at puberty — improves virilization, muscle mass, mood, bone density
  • Speech/language therapy, educational support
  • Fertility: possible with surgical sperm extraction (TESE) + IVF
  • Screen: glucose, lipids, bone density
Board Pearls
  • Most common cause of hypergonadotropic hypogonadism in males
  • FSH and LH ↑ (feedback from failing testes); testosterone ↓
  • Diagnosis: karyotype
  • Gynecomastia at puberty in tall male → think Klinefelter
  • Learning/language difficulties often present before diagnosis
  • Azoospermia on semen analysis → karyotype
Mendelian · Autosomal Dominant
Marfan Syndrome
FBN1 · Chr 15
The tall, stretchy connective tissue disorder — and the aorta is the alarm you cannot ignore.
🎨 Analogy

Fibrillin-1 is the elastic scaffolding material used to build stretchy connective tissues — blood vessel walls, ligaments, lens zonules, skin. In Marfan syndrome, this scaffolding material is defective. Structures that depend on it become too flexible (hypermobile joints, lens subluxation) or too weak to hold pressure (dilating aorta). The aorta — which absorbs thousands of pressure pulses every day — is like a garden hose with a manufacturing defect in the rubber. Over time, it stretches and can catastrophically rupture.

Genetics
  • Autosomal dominant — FBN1 gene mutation (fibrillin-1 protein), chromosome 15
  • ~25% de novo mutations (no family history)
  • Variable expressivity — same family, different severity
  • Ghent nosology criteria used for diagnosis (major + minor criteria across systems)
Classic Features — "MARFAN"
  • Muscular/skeletal: tall stature, long limbs, arachnodactyly, pectus excavatum/carinatum, scoliosis, pes planus, high arched palate
  • Aortic: aortic root dilation → dissection ⭐ — most life-threatening
  • Ratios: arm span > height; lower segment > upper segment
  • Flexibility: joint hypermobility, positive wrist sign (Steinberg), thumb sign
  • A: ectopia lentis (upward lens dislocation) ⭐ — distinguishes from homocystinuria (downward)
  • N: spontaneous pneumothorax (apical blebs)
Cardiovascular — The Priority
  • Aortic root dilation → aortic regurgitation, dissection, rupture
  • MVP (mitral valve prolapse) — common, usually benign
  • Echo at diagnosis then every 6–12 months
  • Avoid contact sports, strenuous exertion, isometric exercise
  • Restrict competitive sports if aortic root enlarged
  • Surgery: prophylactic aortic root replacement when diameter reaches threshold (~4.5–5cm)
Management
  • Beta-blockers (atenolol) or losartan (ARB) — slow aortic root dilation ⭐
  • Annual echo — monitor aortic root
  • Ophthalmology — lens subluxation, myopia, retinal detachment
  • Orthopedics — scoliosis, pectus, pes planus
  • Genetic counseling — 50% transmission
  • Medical alert bracelet (aortic aneurysm risk)
  • Avoid fluoroquinolones (↑ connective tissue risk)
⚠️ Board Comparison — Marfan vs. Homocystinuria
Both have tall stature + arachnodactyly + lens dislocation. Key difference: Marfan = lens dislocates upward; Homocystinuria = lens dislocates downward. Marfan = normal intelligence; Homocystinuria = intellectual disability + thromboembolism risk.
Mendelian · Autosomal Dominant · NF1
Neurofibromatosis Type 1
NF1 · Chr 17
The café-au-lait spot disorder — and the body's way of reminding you that tumors grow where nerves live.
🎨 Analogy

The NF1 gene encodes neurofibromin — a tumor suppressor that acts like a brake pedal on cell growth in Schwann cells and other neural crest derivatives. When the brake is broken, cells along nerves can proliferate unchecked, forming neurofibromas. Meanwhile, the pigmented skin patches (café-au-lait spots) are like the warning lights on a dashboard — they appear early and signal that the underlying braking system has a problem, even before the tumors are visible.

Genetics
  • Autosomal dominant — NF1 gene (neurofibromin tumor suppressor), chromosome 17
  • Most common single-gene disorder: 1:3,000
  • ~50% de novo mutations
  • Variable expressivity — same family, wildly different severity
  • NF2: different gene (chr 22), bilateral acoustic neuromas — less common
Diagnostic Criteria — 2 of 7 Required
  • ☕ ≥6 café-au-lait macules — >5mm prepubertal, >15mm postpubertal ⭐
  • 🦋 ≥2 neurofibromas OR 1 plexiform neurofibroma
  • 👁️ Lisch nodules — iris hamartomas (pathognomonic, seen on slit-lamp)
  • 👁️ Optic glioma — most common CNS tumor in NF1
  • 🦴 Sphenoid dysplasia OR tibial pseudarthrosis
  • 🧬 First-degree relative with NF1
  • 💫 Axillary/inguinal freckling (Crowe's sign) ⭐
Complications & Surveillance
  • Learning disabilities (~50%) — most common complication
  • ADHD, language delays
  • Scoliosis, short stature
  • Plexiform neurofibromas → malignant peripheral nerve sheath tumor (MPNST)
  • Optic pathway glioma → vision loss; MRI brain/orbits
  • ↑ Risk: leukemia (JMML), rhabdomyosarcoma, pheochromocytoma
  • Hypertension: renal artery stenosis or pheochromocytoma
  • Annual: neurological exam, BP, ophthalmology, developmental assessment
Board Pearls
  • Café-au-lait spots + axillary freckling = NF1 until proven otherwise
  • Lisch nodules = pathognomonic (but require slit-lamp — not visible to naked eye)
  • Optic glioma: asymptomatic in many; symptomatic = vision changes, proptosis, precocious puberty
  • Plexiform neurofibroma at birth/infancy → higher malignant transformation risk
  • MEK inhibitor (selumetinib) — FDA approved for plexiform neurofibromas in children
X-Linked · Trinucleotide Repeat
Fragile X Syndrome
FMR1 · CGG repeat · Xq27.3
Most common inherited cause of intellectual disability — and it looks different in boys vs. girls.
🎨 Analogy

The FMR1 gene contains a section of CGG repeating code — like a sentence that normally says "CGG" about 5–54 times. In Fragile X, this stutter expands to over 200 repeats, and the gene gets chemically silenced (methylated). The protein it makes — FMRP, which helps neurons fine-tune synaptic connections — disappears entirely. Without it, synapses become hyperexcitable and disorganized, which is why intellectual disability, autism features, and seizures all emerge. The "fragile" site is where this expanded repeat makes the X chromosome look physically broken under special staining.

Genetics
  • X-linked dominant with variable penetrance
  • FMR1 gene — CGG trinucleotide repeat expansion (Xq27.3)
  • Normal: 5–54 repeats
  • Premutation: 55–200 repeats (carriers; at risk for FXTAS in males, FXPOI in females)
  • Full mutation: >200 repeats → methylation → gene silencing → no FMRP
  • Anticipation — repeat expands with each generation
  • Most common inherited cause of intellectual disability
  • Most common genetic cause of autism spectrum disorder
Classic Features — Males
  • Intellectual disability — mild to severe
  • Autism-like features: hand flapping, poor eye contact, social anxiety, perseverative speech
  • Macroorchidism (large testes) — most reliable post-pubertal finding ⭐
  • Prominent ears, long narrow face, prominent jaw
  • Hyperextensible joints, flat feet
  • Mitral valve prolapse
  • Seizures (~20%)
  • Hypotonia and delayed motor milestones
Features — Females & Diagnosis
  • Females: milder (one normal X); may have learning disabilities, shyness, social anxiety
  • Premutation carrier females: risk of FXPOI (premature ovarian insufficiency) and FXTAS (tremor/ataxia in older age)
  • Diagnosis: FMR1 DNA testing — Southern blot + PCR for repeat number
  • Chromosome analysis will NOT detect it (no visible chromosomal change)
  • Screen any male with unexplained intellectual disability + autism features
Management
  • No cure — supportive and symptomatic
  • Early intervention: speech therapy, OT, behavioral therapy (ABA)
  • Stimulants or guanfacine for ADHD features
  • SSRIs for anxiety
  • Antiepileptics if seizures present
  • Genetic counseling — maternal premutation carriers at risk for expansion in children
  • Screen siblings and extended family (X-linked inheritance)
Non-Mendelian · Genomic Imprinting · Chr 15
Prader-Willi Syndrome
15q11–q13 · Paternal deletion
Floppy hypotonic infant → insatiable hungry toddler — the same chromosome region, but which parent matters.
🎨 Analogy

Chromosome 15q11 is a region where only one parent's copy is expressed — a phenomenon called imprinting. Normally, you use Dad's copy of this region. In Prader-Willi, Dad's copy is missing (deleted or silenced), and Mom's copy of this region is imprinted (silenced). So there's effectively no working copy at all. The hypothalamus, which is supposed to regulate hunger and satiety, loses a critical set of instructions — resulting in the signature hyperphagia. Compare with Angelman syndrome: same region, but when Mom's copy is missing, you get a completely different syndrome (happy, seizures, non-verbal).

Genetics
  • Paternal deletion of 15q11–q13 (70%) — most common
  • Maternal uniparental disomy (UPD) — both chromosome 15s from mom (25%)
  • Imprinting defect (rare)
  • Contrast: Angelman syndrome = maternal deletion of same region = different phenotype
  • Incidence: 1:10,000–30,000
  • Diagnosis: methylation analysis (detects all 3 mechanisms) → FISH or chromosomal microarray to characterize
Biphasic Presentation
  • Phase 1 — Infancy:
    • Profound hypotonia ("floppy baby") ⭐
    • Poor suck → feeding difficulties, FTT
    • Undescended testes (males), hypoplastic genitalia
    • Almond-shaped eyes, thin upper lip
  • Phase 2 — Toddler/childhood:
    • Hyperphagia — insatiable appetite, food-seeking behavior ⭐
    • Rapid obesity if not controlled
    • Short stature (GH deficiency)
    • Mild–moderate intellectual disability
    • Behavioral: temper tantrums, stubbornness, OCD features, skin-picking
Associated Conditions
  • Obesity → T2DM, sleep apnea, orthopedic complications
  • Scoliosis
  • Hypogonadism → delayed/incomplete puberty, infertility
  • GH deficiency → short stature
  • Hypothyroidism
  • Psychiatric: psychosis in adolescence/adulthood
  • High pain threshold — may not express signs of injury/illness
Management
  • Growth hormone therapy — improves height, muscle mass, body composition; start early (even infancy)
  • Strict dietary/caloric control — food access must be supervised; food seeking behavior is uncontrollable without external structure
  • Lock food away — this is a genuine medical necessity, not a preference
  • Early intervention: PT (hypotonia), OT, speech
  • Sex hormone replacement for puberty
  • Behavioral management: structured environment, ABA, mental health support
  • Sleep study (OSA)
  • Scoliosis monitoring
🧬 Imprinting Quick Comparison
Prader-Willi = paternal 15q11 deletion → hypotonia + hyperphagia + obesity + intellectual disability. Angelman = maternal 15q11 deletion → "happy puppet" — severe intellectual disability, absent speech, seizures, ataxia, inappropriate laughter. Same chromosome, opposite parent = opposite syndrome.

Board Quick Quiz · Flip to Reveal
Genetics Flash Cards
tap any card to flip ↓
Newborn with hypotonia, flat facies, upslanting eyes, single palmar crease. Most common cardiac defect?
Down syndrome (Trisomy 21). Most common CHD = AVSD (atrioventricular septal defect / AV canal). Echo at birth is mandatory.
Down syndrome confirmed with translocation. What do you do next?
Karyotype both parents — translocation type has higher recurrence risk (not age-related like nondisjunction). One parent may be a balanced translocation carrier.
15-year-old girl, short stature, no puberty, webbed neck. Karyotype 45,X. What is the most common cardiac defect?
Turner syndrome. Coarctation of the aorta is the most common CHD. Also get renal US (horseshoe kidney), TSH annually, and start GH + estrogen therapy.
Is Turner syndrome associated with advanced maternal age?
NO — Turner syndrome is NOT associated with advanced maternal age. This is a classic board trap. Trisomy 21 and Klinefelter are associated with advanced maternal age.
Tall male, small firm testes, gynecomastia, infertility workup shows azoospermia. Diagnosis?
Klinefelter syndrome (47,XXY). Confirm with karyotype. Start testosterone replacement at puberty. Most common genetic cause of male infertility.
Tall teen with arachnodactyly, pectus excavatum, and lens displaced upward. What medication slows aortic dilation?
Marfan syndrome. Beta-blockers (atenolol) or losartan slow aortic root dilation. Annual echo mandatory. Avoid contact sports.
Marfan vs. Homocystinuria — how do you tell them apart on boards?
Both: tall + arachnodactyly + lens dislocation. Key difference: Marfan = lens UP, normal IQ. Homocystinuria = lens DOWN, intellectual disability, thromboembolism risk.
Child has 7 café-au-lait spots and axillary freckling. What other finding is pathognomonic for this diagnosis?
NF1. Pathognomonic finding = Lisch nodules (iris hamartomas on slit-lamp). Requires 2 of 7 criteria for diagnosis. Café-au-lait + axillary freckling already = 2.
Most common CNS tumor in NF1? Most common complication overall?
Most common CNS tumor = optic glioma. Most common complication overall = learning disabilities (~50%). NF1 is the most common single-gene disorder (1:3,000).
Male with intellectual disability, large ears, autism features, and post-pubertal macroorchidism. Diagnosis and most specific genetic test?
Fragile X syndrome. FMR1 DNA testing (Southern blot + PCR) — NOT standard karyotype. Most common inherited ID and most common genetic cause of autism.
Floppy hypotonic newborn, poor suck, undescended testes. By toddlerhood: obsessive eating, rapid obesity. Diagnosis?
Prader-Willi syndrome. Paternal deletion 15q11. GH therapy + strict dietary control. Lock food — hyperphagia is neurologic, not behavioral choice.
Same chromosomal region as Prader-Willi, but maternal deletion. What syndrome results?
Angelman syndrome — maternal 15q11 deletion. "Happy puppet": severe intellectual disability, absent speech, seizures, ataxia, inappropriate laughter. Completely different from PWS.
