Neurodevelopmental Disorders

Onset in the developmental period (before school age or early childhood). Deficits produce impairments in personal, social, academic, or occupational functioning.

Intellectual Disability (ID)

DSM-5Prevalence ~1%

Definition

Deficits in intellectual functioning (reasoning, problem-solving, abstract thinking) AND adaptive functioning (conceptual, social, practical domains), onset during developmental period.

Severity Levels (based on adaptive functioning)

LevelIQ range (approx)Features
Mild55–70Can live semi-independently; academic delay, concrete thinking
Moderate40–55Needs support for ADLs; limited academic achievement
Severe25–40Requires supervision; limited verbal communication
Profound<25Dependent for all care; often medical comorbidities

Etiology

  • Genetic: Down syndrome, Fragile X, Prader-Willi, Angelman
  • Prenatal: FASD, TORCH infections, maternal malnutrition
  • Perinatal: hypoxic-ischemic injury, prematurity, kernicterus
  • Postnatal: meningitis, TBI, lead exposure

Evaluation

  • Standardized IQ testing (WISC, Stanford-Binet) + adaptive assessment (Vineland)
  • Chromosomal microarray + Fragile X testing
  • Hearing & vision screen, TSH, lead level
🎯 Board PearlIQ alone is no longer sufficient — DSM-5 requires both intellectual and adaptive deficits. Severity is now determined by adaptive functioning, not IQ.

Specific Learning Disorder

DSM-5

Persistent difficulty (≥6 months despite intervention) in reading (dyslexia), written expression (dysgraphia), or mathematics (dyscalculia), substantially below age expectation, not explained by ID, sensory deficits, or lack of schooling.

Management

  • Educational accommodations via IEP or 504 plan
  • Multidisciplinary assessment (psychoeducational testing)
  • Rule out hearing/vision deficits, ADHD, anxiety

Fetal Alcohol Spectrum Disorder (FASD)

PreventableLeading cause of preventable ID

Classic Triad (Fetal Alcohol Syndrome)

  1. Growth deficiency (prenatal + postnatal)
  2. Facial dysmorphism: short palpebral fissures, smooth philtrum, thin upper vermilion
  3. CNS abnormalities: microcephaly, ID, behavioral/cognitive deficits

Management

  • No safe amount of alcohol in pregnancy — prevention is key
  • Early intervention, special education, behavioral therapy
  • Screen for comorbid ADHD (very common)
🧠 Mnemonic"SMOOTH PHILTRUM" — Short palpebral fissures, Microcephaly, Organ defects (heart), Optic issues, Thin upper lip, Hyperactivity, Philtrum smooth, Hearing loss, IQ low, Learning problems, Tremor (neonatal withdrawal), Retardation of growth, Underdeveloped midface, Malformations.

Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)

Prevalence 5–11%M > F

DSM-5 Criteria (summary)

  • ≥6 symptoms of inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity (≥5 if age ≥17)
  • Present <12 years old, in ≥2 settings (home + school), ≥6 months
  • Functional impairment; not better explained by another disorder

Presentations

TypeHallmark
Predominantly InattentiveCareless errors, forgetful, disorganized, distractible (often missed in girls)
Predominantly Hyperactive/ImpulsiveFidgets, can't stay seated, interrupts, blurts out
CombinedBoth — most common in school-age boys

Workup

  • Vanderbilt or Conners rating scales (parent + teacher)
  • Rule out: hearing/vision issue, OSA, thyroid, lead, anxiety, learning disorder
  • Baseline vitals, growth chart, cardiac history (before stimulants)

Management

AgeFirst-line
Preschool (4–5 yr)Behavioral therapy (parent training) FIRST; methylphenidate if severe
School-age (6–11)FDA-approved medication + behavioral therapy
Adolescent (12–18)Medication ± CBT, with assent

Medications

  • Stimulants (first-line): methylphenidate, amphetamine salts. SE: ↓appetite, insomnia, ↑HR/BP, growth slowing, tics, mood lability.
  • Non-stimulants: atomoxetine (SNRI — check LFTs, black-box suicidality), guanfacine/clonidine ER (α2 agonists — sedation, ↓BP).
🏥 Clinical ActionMonitor height, weight, BP, HR at each visit. Drug holidays are optional on weekends for growth. Stop and reassess if chest pain, syncope, or new cardiac symptoms.

Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)

Prevalence ~1 in 36M:F ≈ 4:1

DSM-5 Core Features (both required)

  1. Persistent deficits in social communication & interaction: social-emotional reciprocity, nonverbal communication, relationships
  2. Restricted, repetitive patterns of behavior: stereotypies, insistence on sameness, restricted interests, sensory sensitivities

Onset in early developmental period. Severity graded by level of support needed (Level 1–3).

Red Flags (Bright Futures / AAP)

  • No babbling or gesturing by 12 months
  • No single words by 16 months
  • No 2-word phrases by 24 months
  • ANY loss of language or social skills at any age
  • Lack of eye contact, poor response to name by 12 months

Screening

  • M-CHAT-R/F at 18 and 24 months (AAP)
  • Developmental surveillance at every well-child visit
  • Refer for comprehensive evaluation if positive

Management

  • Early, intensive behavioral intervention (ABA) — best evidence for improving outcomes
  • Speech, occupational, and physical therapy
  • Individualized Education Program (IEP)
  • Meds target comorbidities: risperidone/aripiprazole (FDA-approved for irritability), SSRIs (anxiety), stimulants (ADHD)
🎯 Board PearlASD is a clinical diagnosis — no lab test. Early diagnosis matters: intervention before age 3 has the greatest impact on long-term outcomes.

Eating Disorders

Most common in adolescent girls; now rising in boys. High mortality (especially AN). Early detection in primary care is essential.

DisorderCore FeatureWeightCompensatory Behavior
Anorexia Nervosa (AN)Restriction + intense fear of weight gain + disturbed body imageLow (BMI < expected)Restricting OR binge/purge subtype
Bulimia Nervosa (BN)Recurrent binge eating + compensatory behaviors ≥1x/wk × 3 moNormal or overweightVomiting, laxatives, fasting, exercise
Binge Eating Disorder (BED)Binges ≥1x/wk × 3 mo without compensation; marked distressOften overweight/obeseNone
ARFIDAvoidance/restriction based on sensory, fear of consequences, or low interest — NOT body imageLow or faltering growthNone

Medical Complications of Eating Disorders

  • Cardiac: bradycardia, orthostatic hypotension, prolonged QTc, arrhythmia (leading cause of death in AN)
  • Electrolytes: hypokalemia, hypophosphatemia, hypomagnesemia; metabolic alkalosis (vomiting) or acidosis (laxatives)
  • Endocrine: amenorrhea, osteoporosis, euthyroid sick syndrome, low estrogen/testosterone
  • GI: delayed gastric emptying, constipation, Mallory-Weiss tears, parotid hypertrophy (BN), dental erosion
  • Derm: lanugo, cold intolerance, Russell's sign (knuckle calluses from self-induced vomiting)
🏥 Admit Criteria (AN)HR < 50 (day) or < 45 (night), BP < 80/50, orthostatic Δ HR > 20 or BP > 10, temperature < 96°F, < 75% median BMI, electrolyte abnormality, arrhythmia, syncope, or acute food refusal.

Refeeding Syndrome

Life-threatening shift of phosphate, potassium, and magnesium intracellularly when carbs are reintroduced in a malnourished patient. Can cause cardiac failure, seizures, death.

  • Start low (~20 kcal/kg/day or less), advance slowly
  • Check & replete phosphate, K, Mg before and during refeeding
  • Thiamine supplementation before carbs

Treatment

  • AN (adolescent): Family-Based Treatment (Maudsley method) is first-line. Nutritional rehab. Meds are adjunctive only (olanzapine may help weight gain; SSRIs ineffective until weight restored).
  • BN: CBT-E + fluoxetine 60 mg (only FDA-approved med for BN). Avoid bupropion (↓seizure threshold).
  • BED: CBT, IPT; lisdexamfetamine (FDA-approved), SSRIs, topiramate.
  • ARFID: CBT-AR, feeding therapy, OT for sensory issues.
🧠 Mnemonic"FBT for AN, Fluoxetine for BN" — and bupropion is BANNED in bulimia.

Anxiety & OCD Spectrum

Anxiety disorders are the most common mental health disorders in children. Developmentally normal fears (strangers ~8 mo, separation ~12–18 mo, dark/monsters ~3–5 yr) become disorders when excessive, persistent, and impairing.

Separation Anxiety Disorder

Developmentally inappropriate, excessive fear of separation from attachment figure, ≥4 wks in children. Somatic complaints (HA, stomach ache) on school mornings, nightmares about separation, refusal to sleep alone, school refusal.

Tx: CBT first-line; SSRI (fluoxetine, sertraline) if moderate-severe or CBT insufficient.

Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)

Excessive worry about multiple events, ≥6 months, with ≥1 physical symptom in children (restlessness, fatigue, poor concentration, irritability, muscle tension, sleep disturbance).

Tx: CBT + SSRI (sertraline, fluoxetine, escitalopram) for moderate-severe.

Social Anxiety Disorder

Marked fear of social or performance situations; avoidance of speaking, eating, writing in front of others. In children, anxiety must occur with peers (not only adults).

Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)

Recurrent obsessions (intrusive thoughts) and/or compulsions (repetitive behaviors to reduce anxiety), ≥1 hr/day or causing impairment.

Tx: CBT with Exposure & Response Prevention (ERP) is first-line. SSRIs (fluoxetine, sertraline, fluvoxamine — FDA-approved in peds) often need higher doses than for depression. Clomipramine is second-line.

PANDAS / PANS

Post-infectious

Pediatric Autoimmune Neuropsychiatric Disorder Associated with Streptococcal infection: abrupt (overnight) onset of OCD and/or tics in a prepubertal child following GAS infection. PANS is the broader syndrome (any trigger: Mycoplasma, viral, non-infectious).

Diagnostic Criteria (PANDAS)

  • OCD and/or tic disorder
  • Prepubertal onset
  • Episodic course (abrupt onset/relapses)
  • Temporal association with GAS infection (↑ASO, anti-DNase B)
  • Neurologic signs (choreiform movements)

Tx: Treat active strep with penicillin/amoxicillin; CBT-ERP + SSRI for OCD; refer to specialist for IVIG/steroids in severe cases.

🎯 Board PearlFirst-line for all pediatric anxiety + OCD = CBT. Add SSRI (fluoxetine, sertraline, escitalopram) for moderate-severe. Start low, go slow — watch for behavioral activation and the FDA black-box suicidality warning (monitor weekly × 4 wk, then monthly).

Mood Disorders

Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)

USPSTF screen age 12+

DSM-5 (children/adolescents)

≥5 symptoms for ≥2 weeks, including depressed/irritable mood OR anhedonia:

SIG-E-CAPS: Sleep change, Interest loss, Guilt, Energy loss, Concentration, Appetite change, Psychomotor change, Suicidality.

Screening

  • PHQ-9 Modified for Teens (score ≥10 = moderate; ≥15 = moderately severe)
  • Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale (C-SSRS) for all positive screens

Treatment

SeverityFirst-line
MildActive support, CBT or IPT-A
Moderate-SevereCBT/IPT + SSRI

FDA-approved peds SSRIs:

  • Fluoxetine — age ≥8 for MDD
  • Escitalopram — age ≥12 for MDD
🏥 Black Box WarningSSRIs carry an FDA black-box warning for increased suicidal ideation in patients < 25. Monitor weekly × 4 weeks, then biweekly × 4, then monthly. Discuss openly with family. Benefits outweigh risks when used with monitoring.

Disruptive Mood Dysregulation Disorder (DMDD)

DSM-5 category created to reduce over-diagnosis of pediatric bipolar. Onset age 6–10, diagnosed ages 6–18.

Criteria

  • Severe, recurrent temper outbursts grossly out of proportion, ≥3x/week, ≥12 months
  • Persistently irritable or angry mood between outbursts
  • Present in ≥2 settings

Tx: Parent management training, CBT; SSRI or stimulant if comorbid; atypical antipsychotics reserved for severe.

Bipolar Disorder

True pediatric bipolar is uncommon; classic mania requires distinct episodes of elevated/irritable mood + increased energy ≥1 week (Bipolar I) or ≥4 days hypomania (Bipolar II).

DIG FAST: Distractibility, Impulsivity/Indiscretion, Grandiosity, Flight of ideas, Activity ↑, Sleep ↓, Talkativeness.

Tx: Mood stabilizers (lithium — FDA ≥12; valproate — avoid in girls of reproductive age, teratogenic + PCOS; atypicals: risperidone, aripiprazole, quetiapine, olanzapine FDA-approved in peds).

🎯 Board PearlDo NOT give an SSRI alone to a patient with undiagnosed bipolar — it can precipitate a manic switch. Always screen for past hypomanic/manic episodes before starting an antidepressant in a teen with depression.

Disruptive Behavior Disorders

Oppositional Defiant Disorder (ODD)

Pattern of angry/irritable mood, argumentative/defiant behavior, or vindictiveness lasting ≥6 months, with ≥4 symptoms. Directed at authority figures; rights of others generally NOT violated.

Tx: Parent Management Training (PMT) is first-line; Parent-Child Interaction Therapy (PCIT) for younger children; CBT for older children. Treat comorbid ADHD/anxiety.

Conduct Disorder (CD)

Repetitive, persistent pattern violating the basic rights of others or major age-appropriate societal norms. 4 categories: aggression to people/animals, destruction of property, deceitfulness/theft, serious rule violations. Childhood-onset (<10 yr) worse prognosis than adolescent-onset.

Tx: Multisystemic therapy (MST), functional family therapy, PMT; no FDA-approved medication — treat comorbidities.

🏥 Red FlagCD with callous-unemotional traits ("limited prosocial emotions" specifier) — lack of remorse, shallow affect, lack of empathy — carries highest risk of adult antisocial personality disorder. Aggressive early intervention warranted.

Intermittent Explosive Disorder (IED)

Recurrent, impulsive aggressive outbursts out of proportion to provocation. Minimum age 6. Not better explained by another disorder. Treat with CBT + SSRI.

Child Maltreatment

NPs are mandated reporters. Reasonable suspicion — not proof — triggers a report to Child Protective Services (CPS) in all 50 states.

Types

TypeFindings
Physical abuseBruises in non-ambulatory infant ("those who don't cruise rarely bruise"), patterned injuries, burns with sharp margins, retinal hemorrhages (AHT), metaphyseal/rib fractures
Sexual abuseMost exams are NORMAL. STI in prepubertal child, pregnancy, disclosure, behavioral regression
Emotional abuseFailure to thrive (non-organic), developmental delay, extreme behaviors
NeglectPoor hygiene, untreated medical problems, missed immunizations, school absenteeism
Medical child abuse (formerly MSBP)Caregiver fabricates/induces illness; pattern of doctor-shopping and unexplained findings

Red Flags (Sentinel Injuries)

  • Bruises in a pre-cruising infant (<4–6 mo)
  • Bruises on ears, neck, torso (TEN-4 rule: Torso, Ears, Neck in a child <4 yr, or ANY bruising <4 mo)
  • Inconsistent or changing history
  • Delayed presentation
  • Injury inconsistent with developmental stage
  • Classic metaphyseal lesions, posterior rib fractures, spiral femur fractures in non-ambulatory child

Workup for Suspected Physical Abuse

  • Skeletal survey in children <2 yr
  • Head CT/MRI if AHT suspected (+ dilated fundus exam for retinal hemorrhages)
  • CBC, coags, LFTs, amylase/lipase, UA (for occult injury)
  • Photograph injuries
  • Consult child abuse pediatrician; admit for safety if needed; report to CPS
🧠 MnemonicTEN-4-FACESp — Torso, Ear, Neck bruises in < 4 yr (or any bruise < 4 mo), or Frenulum/Angle of jaw/Cheek/Eyelid/Subconjunctival/Patterned bruises = HIGHLY concerning for abuse.

Substance Use in Adolescents

Adolescence is the highest-risk period for initiation. Earlier onset = higher lifetime SUD risk. Universal screening recommended by AAP starting at age 11.

Screening Tools

  • CRAFFT 2.1 (ages 12–21): Car, Relax, Alone, Forget, Family/Friends, Trouble. ≥2 = positive, needs brief intervention.
  • S2BI (Screening to Brief Intervention): frequency-based, quick.
  • BSTAD (Brief Screener for Tobacco, Alcohol, and other Drugs).

Substances

SubstanceKey Points
AlcoholMost-used. Binge drinking = 4+ (F) or 5+ (M) drinks in 2 hr. Screen with CRAFFT; FDA-approved peds tx none — naltrexone off-label.
Tobacco / VapingE-cigs (JUUL, disposables) now dominate. Nicotine exposure during adolescence impairs prefrontal cortex development. EVALI: lipoid pneumonia from vitamin E acetate. Varenicline/NRT off-label < 18.
CannabisEdibles ↑ ED visits; high-potency concentrates ↑ cannabis-induced psychosis and cannabinoid hyperemesis. Associated with ↓ IQ and ↑ schizophrenia risk if heavy use in adolescence.
OpioidsRx misuse often precedes heroin/fentanyl. Naloxone for every household with risk. Buprenorphine FDA-approved ≥16 yr; methadone via OTP.
StimulantsCocaine, methamphetamine, prescription stimulant misuse. No FDA-approved pharmacotherapy; CBT/contingency management.
OtherInhalants (young adolescents), hallucinogens, MDMA, benzos, GHB (DFSA).

SBIRT Framework

  • Screening: validated tool at every well visit
  • Brief Intervention: motivational interviewing
  • Referral to Treatment: outpatient, IOP, residential per severity
🎯 Board PearlConfidentiality for adolescent substance-use discussions is protected in most states — but break confidentiality for imminent danger (overdose risk, suicidality, driving intoxicated, pregnancy-related concerns per state law).
