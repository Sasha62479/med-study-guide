Heart Failure
ACC/AHA 2022 Guidelines — Classification, Diagnosis, and Management

Definition and Classification

Heart failure (HF) is a clinical syndrome characterized by symptoms and signs caused by a structural or functional cardiac abnormality, resulting in reduced cardiac output or elevated intracardiac pressures.

By EF

HFrEFEF < 40%
HFmrEFEF 41–49%
HFpEFEF ≥ 50%

NYHA Class

Class INo limitation
Class IISlight limitation
Class IIIMarked limitation
Class IVSymptoms at rest

Stages (ACC/AHA)

Stage A

At risk, no structural disease, no symptoms. DM, HTN, obesity, family Hx.

Stage B

Structural/functional disease, no current or prior symptoms.

Stage C

Structural disease with current or prior HF symptoms.

Stage D

Advanced HF; refractory symptoms despite GDMT. Consider advanced therapies.

Common Causes

HFrEF

  • Ischemic cardiomyopathy (most common in US)
  • Dilated cardiomyopathy (idiopathic, viral, toxic)
  • Myocarditis, peripartum cardiomyopathy
  • Prolonged tachycardia, anthracycline toxicity

HFpEF

  • Hypertension (most common cause)
  • Obesity, diabetes, metabolic syndrome
  • Hypertrophic or restrictive cardiomyopathy
  • Atrial fibrillation, aging

Clinical Presentation

Left-sided HF

  • Dyspnea on exertion (earliest)
  • Orthopnea, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea
  • Pulmonary crackles (rales)
  • S3 gallop (volume overload)
  • Fatigue, reduced exercise tolerance

Right-sided HF

  • Peripheral pitting edema
  • Jugular venous distension (JVD)
  • Hepatomegaly, ascites
  • Weight gain from fluid retention
  • Often secondary to left HF

Diagnostic Workup

Initial Labs

  • BNP > 100 pg/mL or NT-proBNP > 300 pg/mL suggests HF
  • CBC, CMP (BUN/Cr), TFTs, lipids, HbA1c
  • Iron studies (iron deficiency common in HF)
  • Urinalysis

Imaging and Studies

  • ECG: assess rhythm, LVH, ischemia, QRS widening
  • Chest X-ray: cardiomegaly, Kerley B lines, cephalization, pleural effusions
  • Echocardiogram: essential to classify EF, assess wall motion, valves
  • Coronary angiography if ischemia suspected

Pharmacologic Management — HFrEF (GDMT)

Goal-directed medical therapy (GDMT) — all four pillars shown to reduce mortality.

Class I Recommendations

The Four Pillars

  • ACEi/ARB or ARNi (sacubitril-valsartan preferred if tolerated)
  • Beta-blocker: carvedilol, metoprolol succinate, or bisoprolol
  • MRA (mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist): spironolactone or eplerenone
  • SGLT2 inhibitor: dapagliflozin or empagliflozin (proven mortality benefit)

Additional Agents

  • Loop diuretics (furosemide) for symptom relief of congestion — not mortality benefit
  • Hydralazine + nitrate: if ACEi/ARB/ARNi not tolerated (especially Black patients)
  • Ivabradine: if HR ≥ 70 bpm on max beta-blocker, sinus rhythm, EF ≤ 35%
  • Digoxin: consider for persistent symptoms, AF (Class IIa)

Management — HFpEF

Fewer mortality-reducing agents proven; focus on symptom control and comorbidities.

  • SGLT2 inhibitors: now recommended (empagliflozin — EMPEROR-Preserved trial)
  • Diuretics for congestion relief
  • Aggressive BP control (< 130/80 mmHg)
  • Rate control for AF; anticoagulation if indicated
  • Treat underlying causes: obesity, DM, sleep apnea, HTN
  • ARB (candesartan) or MRA may reduce hospitalizations (Class IIb)

Device Therapy

  • ICD: EF ≤ 35%, NYHA II-III, on GDMT ≥ 3 months, expected survival > 1 year
  • CRT (cardiac resynchronization): EF ≤ 35%, LBBB with QRS ≥ 150 ms, sinus rhythm, NYHA II-IV
  • LVAD: Stage D HF as bridge-to-transplant or destination therapy
  • Heart transplant: Stage D refractory to all therapies

Acute Decompensated HF (ADHF)

Presentation

  • Rapid worsening dyspnea, hypoxia, severe edema
  • Elevated BNP, chest X-ray with pulmonary congestion

Management (LMNOP)

  • Lasix (IV furosemide) — aggressive diuresis
  • Morphine (use cautiously) — reduces preload/anxiety
  • Nitrates — venodilation, preload reduction
  • Oxygen — supplemental O2, CPAP/BiPAP if needed
  • Position — sit upright, legs dependent

Hemodynamic Profiles

  • Wet-warm: congested, adequate perfusion — diurese
  • Wet-cold: congested, poor perfusion — inotropes + diuresis
  • Dry-cold: no congestion, poor perfusion — IV fluids cautiously, inotropes

Monitoring and Follow-Up

  • Daily weights; instruct patient to call if > 2-3 lb gain in 24 hrs or > 5 lb in 1 week
  • Electrolytes and renal function (BMP) within 1–2 weeks of starting/adjusting diuretics
  • BNP/NT-proBNP to trend disease burden
  • Repeat echo after 3–6 months of GDMT to reassess EF
  • Fluid restriction (1.5–2 L/day) in moderate-severe HF
  • Sodium restriction < 2 g/day
  • Cardiac rehab for stable NYHA II–III

Red Flags / When to Refer or Admit

Admit
  • SpO2 < 90%, respiratory distress at rest
  • SBP < 90 mmHg or signs of hypoperfusion (cool, clammy, altered mental status)
  • New arrhythmia or acute ischemia
  • Acute cardiogenic pulmonary edema
  • Failure of outpatient diuresis
Refer to Cardiology
  • EF < 40%, newly diagnosed HF
  • Advanced HF stage C/D, considering devices or transplant
  • Unexplained or refractory HF

Key Teaching Points

  • HFrEF (EF < 40%) has proven mortality-reducing GDMT — start all four pillars when able
  • SGLT2 inhibitors now benefit both HFrEF and HFpEF
  • ARNi (sacubitril/valsartan) is preferred over ACEi in chronic HFrEF if tolerated
  • Diuretics relieve symptoms but do not reduce mortality
  • Daily weight monitoring is a cornerstone of outpatient HF management
  • BNP helps diagnose HF but also guides prognosis and response to therapy
  • HF with preserved EF is increasingly recognized; SGLT2i now first-line
