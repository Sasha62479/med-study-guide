Cardiovascular System

Heart

A muscular organ in the middle mediastinum that pumps blood through the circulatory system via four chambers, four valves, and a specialized conduction system.

1. Gross Anatomy

Overview

  • Two ventricles and two atria; roughly fist-sized; weighs 300-500 g
  • Surrounded by pericardium (fibroserous, fluid-filled sac)
  • Location: middle mediastinum between the lungs
  • Anterior: sternum and rib cartilage; Posterior: vertebral column (T5-T8), esophagus, carina
  • Upper heart at level of 3rd costal cartilage; Cardiac apex left of sternum at 4th-5th ICS

Cardiac Borders (Frontal View)

  • Right border: right atrium
  • Left border: left ventricle + left atrial appendage
  • Inferior border: right ventricle
  • Superior border: atria and great vessels
CXR: Cardiac borders form the cardiac silhouette on chest X-ray. Right ventricle forms the anterior border on lateral view.

2. Heart Valves

The cardiac skeleton consists of four fibrous rings (annuli fibrosi cordis) that anchor valves, separate atria from ventricles, and provide electrical insulation.

Atrioventricular (AV) Valves

  • Tricuspid valve (3 leaflets): between right atrium and right ventricle
  • Mitral valve/bicuspid valve (2 leaflets): between left atrium and left ventricle
  • Subvalvular apparatus: chordae tendineae (fibrous cords) + papillary muscles (prevent leaflet prolapse during systole)

Semilunar Valves

  • Pulmonary valve (3 cusps): between right ventricle and pulmonary trunk; no subvalvular apparatus
  • Aortic valve (3 cusps): between left ventricle and aorta; aortic sinuses (left and right give rise to coronary arteries); aortic bulb = dilation of root of aorta
Mnemonic: "Try PULling My AORTA" = Tricuspid, Pulmonary, Mitral, Aortic - order blood flows through valves.
Tricuspid = 3 leaflets, Right side (like 3-lobed Right lung). Bicuspid = 2 leaflets, Left side (like 2-lobed Left lung).

3. Coronary Arteries

Coronary blood flow peaks in early diastole. Coronary dominance refers to which artery supplies the posterior descending artery (PDA).

Dominance

  • Right-dominant (~85%): PDA from RCA
  • Left-dominant (~8%): PDA from LCX
  • Codominant (~7%): PDA from both

Venous Drainage

  • Coronary sinus: largest cardiac vein; lies in left posterior AV groove; drains into right atrium
  • Receives: great, posterior, small, and middle cardiac veins
Clinical: RCA stenosis/occlusion often leads to cardiac arrhythmias because it supplies the SA and AV nodes.

4. Conduction System

  • SA node: upper wall of right atrium (SVC junction) - primary pacemaker
  • AV node: within AV septum, superior and medial to coronary sinus opening
  • Bundle of His: below cardiac skeleton in membranous IV septum; branches into left and right bundle branches
  • Purkinje fibers: terminal conducting fibers in subendocardium

5. Microscopic Anatomy

Layers of the Heart Wall

  • Endocardium (innermost): endothelium + loose connective tissue + subendocardium (contains Purkinje cells)
  • Myocardium: cardiomyocytes (striated, single central nucleus, many mitochondria); connected by intercalated discs (adherent junctions = mechanical; gap junctions = electrical)
  • Epicardium: connective tissue = visceral layer of serous pericardium

Pericardium Layers

  • Fibrous pericardium (outermost, stiff)
  • Parietal layer of serous pericardium
  • Pericardial cavity (serous fluid)
  • Visceral layer of serous pericardium (epicardium)
ANP: Atrial cardiomyocytes release atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP) when stretched (high BP) - increases water and Na+ excretion by kidneys - decreases BP.
Clinical: Pericardial tamponade occurs when fluid accumulates in the pericardial space, increasing intrapericardial pressure and impairing cardiac function. Pericarditis can cause referred pain to neck, arms, or shoulders (phrenic nerve innervation).

6. Embryology

Origin: mesoderm (myocardium, pericardium, endocardium, blood vessels).

Key Steps

  • Two endocardial tubes merge to form the heart tube
  • Heart tube loops (S-shape) during week 4 - establishes left-right polarity
  • Endocardial cushions fuse to separate atria and ventricles and form valves
  • Truncus arteriosus divides into aorta and pulmonary trunk via aorticopulmonary septum

7. Clinical Significance

Coronary Artery Disease

  • Acute coronary syndrome (unstable angina, NSTEMI, STEMI), Myocardial infarction

Arrhythmias

  • Atrial fibrillation, Atrial flutter (cavotricuspid isthmus), AV block, Bundle branch blocks, WPW

Heart Failure

  • Systolic vs. diastolic heart failure, Cardiomyopathies (dilated, hypertrophic, restrictive)

Valvular Disease

  • Aortic stenosis/regurgitation, Mitral stenosis/regurgitation/prolapse, Tricuspid/pulmonary valve disease

Pericardial Disease

  • Pericarditis, Pericardial effusion, Cardiac tamponade (Beck's triad: hypotension, JVD, muffled heart sounds)

Congenital Heart Defects

  • Cyanotic: Tetralogy of Fallot, Transposition of great vessels, Truncus arteriosus, Tricuspid atresia
  • Acyanotic: VSD (most common), ASD, PDA, Coarctation of aorta

Other

  • Infective endocarditis, Rheumatic heart disease, Myocarditis, Cardiac myxoma
