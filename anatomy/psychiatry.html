Brain Overview

The brain regions most relevant to psychiatric illness are the prefrontal cortex (executive function, impulse control), the limbic system (emotion, memory, fear), the basal ganglia (habit, reward, movement), the hypothalamus & brainstem (autonomic, stress, sleep), and the monoaminergic nuclei of the brainstem (locus coeruleus, raphe, VTA, substantia nigra) that project widely.

Prefrontal Cortex (PFC)

The PFC is the brain's executive — it plans, inhibits impulses, and regulates emotion by top-down control of the limbic system. It's the last brain region to mature (not fully myelinated until ~25 years old), which is why adolescents are biologically primed for risk-taking.

Subdivisions

  • Dorsolateral PFC (DLPFC) — working memory, cognitive control. Hypoactive in depression and schizophrenia (negative symptoms).
  • Ventromedial PFC (vmPFC) — emotional decision-making, value. Damaged in classic case of Phineas Gage → disinhibition.
  • Orbitofrontal cortex (OFC) — reward evaluation, social behavior. Dysfunction → OCD, impulse disorders.
🎯 Board PearlPFC maturation lag explains why adolescents make impulsive decisions despite knowing risks — the limbic "gas pedal" (amygdala/VTA) develops years before the cortical "brake" (PFC). Clinical implication: adolescent substance use has outsized neurotoxic effects on the still-developing PFC.

Limbic System

The limbic system is a ring of structures around the brainstem that processes emotion, memory, and motivation. Papez circuit: hippocampus → fornix → mammillary bodies → anterior thalamus → cingulate → back to hippocampus.

Amygdala

Almond-shaped cluster in the anterior temporal lobe. The brain's threat detector — tags stimuli with emotional significance, especially fear.

Key Functions

  • Fear conditioning (Pavlovian)
  • Fight-or-flight initiation (projects to hypothalamus → sympathetic activation)
  • Consolidation of emotional memory (with hippocampus)

Clinical Correlations

  • Hyperactive in anxiety, PTSD, panic disorder
  • Hypoactive / lesioned → Klüver-Bucy syndrome (hyperorality, hypersexuality, docility, visual agnosia)
  • SSRIs and benzodiazepines reduce amygdala reactivity

Hippocampus

Seahorse-shaped structure in the medial temporal lobe. Essential for declarative (explicit) memory formation and spatial navigation.

Clinical Correlations

  • Bilateral damage → anterograde amnesia (classic: patient H.M.)
  • Atrophy in chronic depression, PTSD, Cushing's, Alzheimer's
  • One of two regions with adult neurogenesis — SSRIs promote hippocampal neurogenesis (hypothesized mechanism of delayed antidepressant response)
🏥 Clinical ActionThe ~2–4 week delay before SSRIs work may reflect the time needed for hippocampal neurogenesis and synaptic remodeling. Counsel patients: "The medication may not feel like it's working for 2–4 weeks. Don't stop early."

Anterior Cingulate Cortex (ACC)

Wraps around the corpus callosum. Bridges cognition and emotion. Two functional subdivisions:

  • Dorsal ACC — cognitive control, error monitoring, attention (active during Stroop task)
  • Subgenual ACC (Area 25) — affective regulation. Hyperactive in depression; target of deep brain stimulation for treatment-resistant MDD.

Basal Ganglia

Cluster of subcortical nuclei (caudate, putamen, globus pallidus, subthalamic nucleus, substantia nigra) involved in movement, habit, and reward.

StructurePsych Relevance
CaudateHyperactive in OCD (cortico-striatal-thalamic loop); atrophies in Huntington's
PutamenHabit learning; involved in tics/Tourette's
Nucleus accumbens (ventral striatum)Reward and addiction hub — terminal of mesolimbic dopamine
Substantia nigra pars compactaSource of nigrostriatal dopamine → movement. Degenerates in Parkinson's. Blocked by antipsychotics → EPS.
🧠 Mnemonic"OCD loops the caudate" — the cortico-striatal-thalamo-cortical (CSTC) circuit is hyperactive in OCD. SSRIs and CBT-ERP both normalize this loop on fMRI.

Thalamus & Hypothalamus

Thalamus

Relay station for nearly all sensory info to cortex (except smell). "Gating" dysfunction is implicated in schizophrenia (sensory-gating deficits explain overwhelming stimulation).

Hypothalamus

Master regulator of homeostasis: temperature, appetite, thirst, circadian rhythm, autonomic tone, and the HPA axis.

NucleusFunction
SuprachiasmaticCircadian pacemaker — disturbed in depression, bipolar, shift-work disorder
Ventromedial (VMH)Satiety — "VentroMedial = Very Much Full"
Lateral (LH)Hunger — "Lateral = Lean" (lesion causes anorexia)
Paraventricular (PVN)Releases CRH → drives HPA axis
ArcuateLeptin/ghrelin sensing; disrupted in eating disorders

HPA Axis — The Stress Highway

The central stress-response system, dysregulated in depression, PTSD, anxiety, and chronic illness.

Pathway

  1. Stress perceived → hypothalamic PVN releases CRH
  2. CRH → anterior pituitary → ACTH
  3. ACTH → adrenal cortex → cortisol
  4. Cortisol negative-feedback at hypothalamus & pituitary

Pathologic States

  • Depression — HPA hyperactivity, flattened diurnal cortisol curve, blunted dexamethasone suppression
  • PTSD — paradoxically LOW cortisol with enhanced negative feedback
  • Chronic stress — hippocampal atrophy (cortisol neurotoxicity), amygdala hypertrophy
  • Cushing's — psych symptoms in ~50% (depression, mania, psychosis, cognitive decline)
🎯 Board PearlAlways screen for Cushing's in atypical or treatment-resistant depression — especially with central obesity, easy bruising, proximal weakness, or hypertension/hyperglycemia. Check 24-hr urine cortisol or late-night salivary cortisol.

Reward Circuitry — The Mesolimbic System

Dopaminergic pathway from VTA (ventral tegmental area) → nucleus accumbens → prefrontal cortex. This circuit encodes "wanting" and motivation, and is hijacked by every addictive drug.

Four Dopamine Pathways

PathwayFrom → ToFunctionDysfunction
MesolimbicVTA → NAccReward, motivation↑ = positive sx of schizophrenia, addiction
MesocorticalVTA → PFCExecutive, cognition↓ = negative sx of schizophrenia
NigrostriatalSNc → striatumMovement↓ = Parkinson's, EPS from D2 blockers
TuberoinfundibularHypothalamus → pituitaryInhibits prolactinBlocked by antipsychotics → hyperprolactinemia
🧠 Mnemonic"4 M's of dopamine": Mesolimbic (Motivation), Mesocortical (Mind/cognition), Movement (nigrostriatal), Milk-inhibiting (tuberoinfundibular).

Default Mode Network (DMN)

A set of regions active when the mind is at rest and self-referential — medial PFC, posterior cingulate, precuneus, angular gyrus. Involved in rumination, self-reflection, and mind-wandering.

  • Hyperactive DMN — depression (rumination), anxiety (worry)
  • Disrupted connectivity — schizophrenia, autism
  • Mindfulness-based therapies and psychedelics (ketamine, psilocybin) quiet DMN overactivity
