Anatomy & Physiology

Liver

The liver is a wedge-shaped organ located beneath the diaphragm in the right upper quadrant. It plays a central role in metabolism, synthesis, detoxification, and storage.

1. Gross Anatomy

General Structure

  • Largest gland in the body; weight: 2.6-3.3 lb in adults
  • Wedge-shaped; four lobes: Right (largest), Left, Quadrate, Caudate
  • Divided into 8 segments (Couinaud classification)
  • Hepatic capsule: outer serous layer (peritoneum) + Glisson capsule (inner fibrous)

Porta Hepatis Structures

  • Common hepatic duct (leaving)
  • Hepatic artery proper (entering)
  • Hepatic portal vein (entering)
  • Hepatic nerve plexus and lymphatic vessels

Ligaments

  • Falciform ligament: connects liver to abdominal wall; contains round ligament
  • Hepatoduodenal ligament: connects liver to duodenum; contains portal triad
  • Gastrohepatic ligament: connects liver to lesser curvature of stomach; contains gastric arteries
Clinical Correlate: In liver hemorrhage, temporary clamping of the hepatoduodenal ligament (Pringle maneuver) achieves hemostasis.

  • Pain refers to: right shoulder, upper right quadrant, epigastric region

2. Microscopic Anatomy

Hepatic Lobules

The liver is divided into hexagonal lobules, each with a central hepatic vein and portal triads at the vertices. Each portal triad = branch of hepatic artery + branch of portal vein + bile ductule + lymphatics + vagus nerve branch.

Parenchyma and Sinusoids

  • Mostly hepatocytes with large amounts of smooth ER (detoxification)
  • Hepatic sinusoids: large capillaries with highly fenestrated endothelium
  • Kupffer cells (macrophages) in sinusoids: phagocytize bacteria, foreign particles, old RBCs
  • Space of Disse: contains hepatic stellate cells (Ito cells) - store vitamin A; main source of fibrosis in liver injury

Energy metabolismGlycolysis, beta-oxidation, amino acid degradation, lipid metabolism, LDL receptors, HDL/VLDL handling

Note: The liver cannot use ketone bodies for itself - hepatocytes lack beta-ketoacyl-CoA transferase (thiophorase).
Fasting: Day 1 = glycogen for gluconeogenesis. Days 1-3 (after glycogen depleted) = free fatty acids for energy.

4. Ethanol Metabolism

Zero-order elimination (~1 oz/hour). NAD+ is the limiting reactant.

Pathway

  • Ethanol to Acetaldehyde (alcohol dehydrogenase)
  • Acetaldehyde to Acetate (acetaldehyde dehydrogenase)
  • Acetate + CoA to Acetyl-CoA (thiokinase, uses ATP)
Disulfiram: Inhibits acetaldehyde dehydrogenase, causing acetaldehyde buildup and hangover symptoms. Used in alcohol use disorder treatment.
Mnemonic: It is DISgusting to drink alcohol when taking DISulfiram!

Consequences of Heavy Ethanol Use (increased NADH/NAD+ ratio)

  • Lactic acidosis: increased pyruvate to lactate conversion
  • Ketoacidosis: TCA cycle inhibited, acetyl-CoA shunted to ketogenesis
  • Fasting hypoglycemia: impaired gluconeogenesis
  • Hepatosteatosis: increased triglyceride synthesis and accumulation
Sex differences: Females more susceptible to ethanol intoxication - lower total body water and lower alcohol dehydrogenase activity.

5. Embryology

  • Origin: Endoderm
  • Derived from junction of foregut and midgut
  • Ligamentum teres: formed from obliterated umbilical vein; in free edge of falciform ligament

6. Clinical Significance

Hepatic Inflammation

  • Alcoholic hepatitis, NASH, Autoimmune hepatitis, Fitz-Hugh-Curtis syndrome

Hepatic Infections

  • Viral hepatitis, liver abscesses (Klebsiella, amebic), parasitic (P. vivax/ovale hypnozoites reside in liver)

Hepatic Tumors

  • Hepatocellular carcinoma, Angiosarcoma (arsenic/vinyl chloride), Colon cancer metastases, Hepatic adenoma, Hydatid cyst

Hereditary Diseases

  • Hemochromatosis: iron overload
  • Wilson disease: copper overload
  • Von Gierke's disease: increased liver glycogen
  • Dubin-Johnson syndrome: black liver

Others

  • Fatty liver, Hepatomegaly, Budd-Chiari syndrome, Ischemia (Zone 3), Cirrhosis
